Doing Scrum In A Big Way

Scaling Estimation Across Multiple Teams

Agile Project Estimation • 2-Day Workshop

Welcome to Module 6, where we tackle one of the most challenging aspects of Agile estimation: scaling. Up to this point, we have been discussing estimation concepts within a single team. But what happens when you have 5 teams? 10? 50? The math changes, the communication patterns change, and the entire estimation approach needs to adapt. This module examines team metrics at scale, how to determine the right number of teams, and the critical question of backlog architecture. These decisions fundamentally shape how estimation works across your organization.

Module Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Identify and track team-level metrics that support healthy estimation at scale
  • Explain why cross-team velocity comparison is misleading and what to use instead
  • Evaluate scaling frameworks (SAFe, LeSS, Nexus) and their impact on estimation
  • Apply Brooks' Law and team topology principles to team sizing decisions
  • Design a backlog architecture that supports multi-team estimation and delivery
  • Describe integration ceremonies that keep scaled teams aligned
Walk through each objective. Emphasize that this module bridges the single-team estimation concepts from earlier modules into multi-team reality. Most organizations will encounter scaling challenges once a product grows beyond what a single Scrum team can handle. The estimation practices we have covered so far still apply, but they need to be adapted and coordinated. This module gives participants the mental models to make those adaptations effectively.

📊 What Are the Team Metrics?

Team-Level Velocity Tracking

Velocity Fundamentals

  • Definition: Story points completed per sprint by a single team
  • Measurement window: Track over 3-5 sprints to establish a reliable range
  • Use it for: Sprint planning capacity and release forecasting
  • Not for: Performance evaluation or cross-team comparison

Healthy velocity pattern:

A stabilized team shows a velocity range, not a fixed number. For example: 28-35 points per sprint. Use the average for planning, but acknowledge the range for risk management.

Watch out for: Velocity used as a target or KPI. Teams will inflate estimates to "hit the number," destroying the metric's usefulness.

Velocity is the most commonly tracked Agile metric, and unfortunately the most commonly misused. Emphasize that velocity is a planning tool, not a performance tool. The moment you start rewarding teams for higher velocity, they will simply inflate their story point estimates. A team that estimates a task at 5 points one sprint will start calling it an 8 the next. The number goes up, but no more work gets done. Velocity only works as a planning input when teams have the psychological safety to estimate honestly. Discuss the concept of a velocity range rather than a single number, and how the range itself is informative about team predictability.

📊 What Are the Team Metrics?

Cross-Team Velocity: Why You Cannot Compare

Metric Team Alpha Team Beta Interpretation
Velocity 45 pts/sprint 28 pts/sprint Incomparable — different calibration
Team Size 8 people 5 people Larger teams naturally produce more points
Point Scale 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13 1, 2, 3, 5, 8 Different scales yield different totals
Domain Frontend UI Backend infrastructure Different complexity baselines

The Cardinal Rule: Story points are a team-internal measure. Each team calibrates points to their own context, skill set, and reference stories. Comparing Team Alpha's 45 to Team Beta's 28 is like comparing kilometers to miles — the units look similar but measure differently.

This is one of the most important messages in the entire module. Walk through the table row by row. Team Alpha has 45 velocity and Team Beta has 28 - does that mean Alpha is more productive? Absolutely not. They have different team sizes, different point scales, and work in entirely different domains. A 5-point story for a frontend team might represent a completely different level of effort than a 5-point story for an infrastructure team. Each team's point scale is internally consistent but externally meaningless. When managers try to compare velocities across teams, it creates perverse incentives and destroys trust. Ask the class: has anyone experienced velocity being used as a performance comparison tool? What happened?

📊 What Are the Team Metrics?

Normalized Story Points & Aggregate Metrics

⚠️ Normalized Story Points

  • Attempt to create a "universal" point scale across teams
  • Requires calibration sessions with reference stories
  • Dangers: Creates false precision, encourages comparison, adds overhead
  • Teams lose ownership of their estimation process
vs

✅ Aggregate Velocity

  • Sum of all team velocities for rough program-level forecasting
  • Useful when teams have similar calibration (e.g., after SAFe PI Planning)
  • Benefits: Simple, directional, low overhead
  • Works best as a trend, not an absolute number

Practical approach: If you must aggregate, use story count (number of stories completed) rather than story points. Story count is less susceptible to calibration differences, as long as teams follow similar story-splitting practices.

Normalized story points are a tempting idea that rarely works well in practice. The concept is to have all teams agree on what a "5" means so you can add up points across teams. The problem is that this agreement is fragile. It requires constant recalibration, it removes teams' autonomy over their own estimation process, and it creates a false sense of precision. If Team Alpha says something is a 5 and Team Beta also says 5, management assumes those represent equal effort, but they rarely do. The better approach for program-level forecasting is aggregate velocity used as a trend line. If the sum of all team velocities has been around 200 for the last few PIs, you can plan around that range. Another option is to count stories rather than points, which sidesteps the calibration problem entirely as long as teams are splitting stories to roughly similar sizes.

📊 What Are the Team Metrics?

Team Health Metrics & the Scrum of Scrums

🎯

Predictability

Planned vs. delivered story points. Target: 80-100% consistently.

😊

Team Happiness

Regular team morale surveys. Trend matters more than score.

🐛

Quality (Defect Rate)

Escaped defects per sprint. Correlates with sustainable pace.

⏱️

Cycle Time

Start to done per story. Identifies bottlenecks.

🔄

Sprint Stability

Scope changes during sprint. High churn signals planning issues.

🤝

Dependency Health

Cross-team blockers resolved on time. Critical at scale.

The Scrum of Scrums: A coordination ceremony where one representative from each team shares: (1) What did we accomplish? (2) What are we doing next? (3) What impediments affect other teams? This is where dependency health metrics become visible at the program level.

Beyond velocity, healthy teams track a constellation of metrics that paint a fuller picture. Predictability tells you how well the team estimates and plans. Team happiness is a leading indicator of future productivity problems. Quality measured through escaped defects reveals whether teams are cutting corners to maintain velocity. Cycle time is arguably more actionable than velocity because it shows where work gets stuck. Sprint stability measures mid-sprint scope changes, and at scale, dependency health becomes critical because blocked teams cascade delays. The Scrum of Scrums ceremony is where these metrics surface at the program level. Emphasize that the Scrum of Scrums is not a status meeting. It is a coordination and impediment-removal session focused on cross-team dependencies. In large organizations, this ceremony is the first line of defense against integration surprises.

🏢 How Many Teams?

Scaling Frameworks Overview

🚀 SAFe

Scaled Agile Framework

  • 5-12 teams per Agile Release Train (ART)
  • PI Planning every 8-12 weeks
  • Prescriptive roles & ceremonies
50-125 people per ART

🌱 LeSS

Large-Scale Scrum

  • 2-8 teams sharing one product backlog
  • One Product Owner, minimal extra roles
Up to ~60 people

🔗 Nexus

Scaled Professional Scrum

  • 3-9 teams building one product
  • Nexus Integration Team coordinates
  • Focus on reducing dependencies
Up to ~70 people

Key insight: The framework you choose directly impacts how estimation works at scale. SAFe normalizes estimation across teams; LeSS keeps team-level estimation intact; Nexus focuses on integration risk.

Give a high-level overview of each framework without going too deep, since this is an estimation course, not a scaling frameworks course. The key point is that each framework makes different trade-offs that affect estimation. SAFe provides the most structure and explicitly addresses estimation through PI Planning, where teams collectively estimate and commit. SAFe is best for large enterprises needing structure. LeSS takes a minimalist approach, keeping Scrum mostly unchanged and letting teams self-organize around a shared backlog. LeSS is best for organizations valuing simplicity. Nexus is somewhere in between, with a dedicated integration team that helps coordinate. Nexus is best for mid-size multi-team products. Ask the class which frameworks they have encountered. Many organizations implement a hybrid or customized version. The important thing is understanding how each approach handles cross-team estimation and planning.

🏢 How Many Teams?

Feature Teams vs. Component Teams

👥 Feature Teams

  • Cross-functional: can deliver end-to-end features
  • Aligned to customer value streams
  • Estimation benefit: Each team estimates and delivers whole stories independently
  • Challenge: Requires broad skill sets; knowledge duplication across teams
vs

⚙️ Component Teams

  • Specialized: owns a specific layer or service
  • Aligned to architecture (frontend, backend, DB)
  • Estimation challenge: A single feature requires estimates from multiple teams
  • Challenge: High dependency overhead; integration risk

Impact on estimation: Component teams turn every feature into a multi-team coordination problem. A story that one feature team can estimate as "8 points" becomes three separate stories across three component teams, each needing coordination. The total effort is almost always greater than the sum of parts due to integration and communication overhead.

This is a critical architectural decision that directly impacts estimation complexity. Feature teams are generally preferred in modern Agile practice because they reduce dependencies. When a feature team estimates a user story, they are estimating the whole thing. They have all the skills needed to deliver it. With component teams, a single user story might require work from the frontend team, the API team, and the database team. Each team estimates their portion, but nobody estimates the integration work. The coordination cost is often invisible in the estimates but very real in execution. Ask the class: if you have three component teams and each estimates their portion of a feature at 5 points, is the total effort 15 points? Almost never. The actual effort is typically 20-25 points once you account for handoffs, integration testing, and communication overhead.

🏢 How Many Teams?

Brooks' Law & Optimal Team Size

📐 Brooks' Law

"Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later."

The number of communication channels in a team:

C = n(n - 1) / 2
5 people10 channels
7 people21 channels
9 people36 channels
12 people66 channels
15 people105 channels

👥 Optimal Team Size: 5-9 people

The Scrum Guide recommends 10 or fewer. Research consistently shows 5-9 as the sweet spot where communication overhead stays manageable and skills are sufficient for cross-functional delivery.

🧠 Dunbar's Number & Scaling

  • 5: Close collaborators (one team)
  • 15: Trusted group (2-3 teams)
  • 50: Extended group (one tribe/ART)
  • 150: Dunbar's limit (max for meaningful relationships)

These cognitive limits are why scaling frameworks cap program sizes at roughly 125 people.

Brooks' Law is from Fred Brooks' 1975 book The Mythical Man-Month. The communication formula is powerful because it shows exponential growth. Going from 5 to 9 people nearly quadruples the communication channels. This is why estimation accuracy tends to decrease in larger teams - there is more miscommunication, more assumptions, and more coordination overhead that is hard to estimate. Dunbar's number, from anthropological research, suggests humans can maintain about 150 meaningful social relationships. This maps remarkably well to scaling framework limits. SAFe ARTs cap at about 125 people. Beyond that, you need another layer of structure. When planning team count and size, these are the fundamental constraints you are working within. It is not about being rigid with numbers; it is about recognizing the cognitive and communication limits that affect team performance and estimation accuracy.

🏢 How Many Teams?

When to Split vs. When to Grow Teams

✂️ Signals to Split a Team

  • Team exceeds 9 members
  • Sprint planning takes too long due to scope breadth
  • Velocity is stable but backlog growth outpaces delivery
  • Distinct sub-domains emerging within the team's work
  • Standup meetings feel like status reports rather than coordination

📈 Signals to Grow (Not Split)

  • Team lacks a critical skill (e.g., no QA, no UX)
  • Team is under 5 members and struggling with absences
  • Work is tightly coupled and splitting would create excessive dependencies
  • Splitting would produce two teams too small to be self-sufficient
  • Onboarding time for new domain context is very high

Estimation impact of splitting: When you split a team, expect velocity to decrease for 3-5 sprints as teams re-form, re-calibrate their estimation baselines, and establish new norms. The total velocity of two new teams will initially be less than the original team. Plan for this dip.

This is a very practical slide for participants. The decision to split or grow a team has real estimation consequences. When you split, you reset the team's estimation history. Each new team needs to recalibrate, and for several sprints, total output will actually decrease. This is Tuckman's model in action: forming, storming, norming, performing. Budget for it. A common mistake is splitting a team right before a critical release because you think more teams means more throughput. In the short term, it means less. The growth path has its own challenges. Adding people to an existing team temporarily slows it down as the new members get up to speed, and the communication channels increase. The key question is always: where does the constraint lie? Is it skill gaps (grow) or scope breadth (split)?

📋 How Many Product Backlogs?

One Backlog per Product — The LeSS Approach

📖 Single Backlog Model

Principle: One product = one backlog = one Product Owner who sets priorities for all teams.

How it works: Teams pull from the same prioritized backlog during sprint planning. Each team selects the highest-priority items they can deliver.

Estimation approach: Teams estimate only the items they will work on, using their own scales. The Product Owner sees the full backlog ordered by value, not by team assignment.

Advantage: Maximum flexibility in prioritization. The highest-value work always gets done first, regardless of which team picks it up. No "my backlog, your backlog" silos.

Challenge: The PO becomes a bottleneck beyond ~4 teams. Requires mature, self-organizing teams that can negotiate work among themselves.

The single backlog model is the simplest conceptually, and it is what LeSS advocates. One Product Owner maintains one ordered backlog. During sprint planning, all teams gather and select work from the top of the backlog. This maximizes value delivery because the highest-priority items are always worked on first. From an estimation perspective, this is clean: each team estimates the items they select using their own calibration. There is no need for normalized points because you are not comparing teams. The main challenges are scale-related. The Product Owner needs to understand all the work across all teams, which becomes impossible beyond 4-6 teams. Also, teams need to be true feature teams capable of working on any part of the product. If teams are specialized, they cannot pull freely from the backlog. LeSS Huge addresses this by introducing Area Product Owners, each managing a portion of the backlog for their area.

📋 How Many Product Backlogs?

Multiple Backlogs with Integration — The SAFe Approach

SAFe Backlog Hierarchy

🎯 Portfolio Backlog
Epics & strategic themes
➡️
📋 Program Backlog
Features for the ART
➡️
📝 Team Backlogs
Stories per team

Program-Level Estimation

  • Features are estimated in normalized story points or T-shirt sizes at the program level
  • PI Planning: Teams decompose features into stories and estimate capacity
  • Program Predictability Measure = planned vs. actual business value per PI

Team-Level Estimation

  • Each team maintains its own backlog of stories decomposed from features
  • Teams estimate using their own velocity and point scale
  • Team velocity feeds into program-level capacity planning

Trade-off: The multi-level backlog structure adds ceremony and overhead, but it enables portfolio-level visibility and alignment across dozens of teams. The estimation happens at multiple levels: T-shirt sizes for epics, normalized points for features, and team-specific points for stories.

SAFe takes the opposite approach from LeSS by introducing multiple backlog levels. This creates a hierarchy where strategic work flows down from the portfolio to the program to individual teams. Each level has its own estimation approach. At the portfolio level, epics are estimated with rough cost-of-delay and WSJF prioritization. At the program level, features are estimated using normalized story points so that the Release Train Engineer and Product Management can plan capacity across teams. At the team level, each team uses its own velocity and story points. PI Planning is the keystone ceremony where all of this comes together. Teams break features into stories, estimate them, identify dependencies, and commit to PI objectives. It is a two-day event that provides alignment and commitment across all teams. The overhead is real but, for large organizations, the alignment it provides can be worth it. Ask participants about their experience with PI Planning if any have done SAFe.

📋 How Many Product Backlogs?

Dependency Management & Shared Services

🔗 Dependency Types

  • Knowledge: "We need Team B's expertise to estimate this"
  • Task: "We cannot start until Team B finishes their API"
  • Resource: "We share a DBA with two other teams"

The dependency tax: Every cross-team dependency adds an estimated 15-30% overhead to the combined effort. Account for this in program-level estimation.

🏗️ Platform & Shared Services Teams

  • Platform teams provide infrastructure, APIs, and tools for other teams
  • Backlog driven by internal customers (other teams)
  • Reserve capacity (e.g., 20%) for platform requests each sprint

📅 Managing Across Backlogs

  • Use a dependency board to visualize cross-team blockers
  • Assign buffer stories in case dependent work is delayed
Dependencies are the silent killer of estimation accuracy at scale. When teams estimate in isolation, they rarely account for the overhead of coordinating with other teams. A story that seems like a 5-pointer when estimated by one team becomes an 8 once you factor in waiting for another team's API, attending cross-team design sessions, and integration testing. The 15-30% overhead figure is a common rule of thumb from scaling practitioners. Additional dependency type not shown on slide: Requirement dependencies, where stories share acceptance criteria with another team's stories. Platform teams present a unique challenge. Their work does not directly deliver features, so it is hard to value-rank against feature work. The estimation challenge is that platform work enables but does not directly deliver business features. The solution is usually to reserve a percentage of their capacity for supporting feature teams. The dependency board is a critical artifact at scale. SAFe uses a program board during PI Planning to visualize dependencies. LeSS achieves this through multi-team sprint planning. Also important: flag dependencies during sprint planning and refinement. Whatever approach you use, making dependencies visible is the first step to managing them. Invisible dependencies are the ones that blow up your estimates.

📋 How Many Product Backlogs?

Integration Ceremonies: Keeping It All Together

Ceremony Frequency Purpose Estimation Impact
📋 PI Planning Every 8-12 weeks Align teams on objectives & capacity Teams estimate features and commit to PI goals
🤝 Scrum of Scrums 2-3x per week Surface cross-team impediments Early warning on at-risk dependencies
🔍 Overall Retro End of PI/release System-level improvement Calibrate accuracy, refine buffers
🔧 Cross-Team Refinement Weekly or as needed Joint refinement of shared stories Reduces estimation variance across teams
🎯 System Demo End of each sprint Demo integrated increment Reveals gaps missed in team estimates

Rule of thumb: The number of integration ceremonies scales with the number of dependencies between teams, not the number of teams. Two tightly coupled teams may need more coordination than five loosely coupled teams.

Walk through each ceremony and its role in maintaining estimation accuracy at scale. PI Planning is the big one in SAFe. It is where all teams in an ART come together for two days to plan the next Program Increment. Teams estimate features, identify dependencies on a program board, and negotiate commitments. The output is a set of PI Objectives with business value assigned by the Product Owner. Scrum of Scrums is the ongoing coordination mechanism. Cross-team refinement is often overlooked but critical. When a feature spans multiple teams, having them refine together ensures they share the same understanding and their estimates are compatible. The System Demo at the end of each sprint is where you discover whether the integrated increment actually works. This is where hidden integration costs become visible. The overall retrospective at the PI level is where teams collectively improve their estimation practices and adjust buffers based on actual results.
🧪 Hands-On Lab

Lab 6: Scaling Estimation Simulation

Apply multi-team estimation in a realistic scaling scenario

  • Form 3-4 Scrum teams of 3-5 participants each
  • Each team receives a shared product backlog with 15-20 features
  • Conduct a simulated PI Planning session:
    • Estimate features using your team's own story points
    • Identify and map cross-team dependencies
    • Create a program board showing delivery timeline
  • Calculate aggregate velocity and forecast a release date
  • Discuss: Where did your estimates account for dependency overhead? Where did they miss?
⏰ Time: 45 minutes  |  📦 Materials: Feature cards, dependency strings, team boards  |  🎯 Goal: Experience the challenges of multi-team estimation firsthand
This lab is the centerpiece of the module. Break participants into 3-4 teams. Give each team a set of feature cards from a shared product backlog. The features should have built-in dependencies between them. Have teams estimate independently first, then come together for a mini PI Planning session. They need to map dependencies on a shared program board using string or tape to connect dependent features. Then they calculate aggregate velocity and try to forecast when the full set of features will be delivered. The debrief is the most important part. Ask: Did any team discover a dependency during planning that changed their estimate? Did any feature take longer to estimate because it spanned teams? How confident are you in the aggregate forecast versus your individual team forecasts? This lab makes the abstract concepts of scaling estimation very concrete.

💡 Key Takeaways

📊 Velocity is team-specific

Never compare velocities across teams. Each team's story points are calibrated to their own context. Use velocity for planning within a team, not for performance evaluation.

👥 Team health drives estimation quality

Track predictability, happiness, quality, and cycle time alongside velocity. Healthy teams produce reliable estimates; stressed teams produce optimistic ones.

🏢 Communication overhead is real

Brooks' Law and Dunbar's Number set hard limits on team size. Keep teams at 5-9 people. Expect a velocity dip when splitting teams.

🔗 Dependencies are the hidden cost

Every cross-team dependency adds 15-30% overhead. Make dependencies visible. Choose feature teams over component teams when possible.

📋 Backlog architecture shapes estimation

One backlog (LeSS) maximizes flexibility. Multiple backlogs (SAFe) enable portfolio visibility. Choose based on organizational needs and team maturity.

📅 Integration ceremonies are non-negotiable

PI Planning, Scrum of Scrums, and cross-team refinement are how scaled estimation stays aligned. Without them, teams estimate in silos and integration surprises follow.

Review each takeaway and tie it back to the course objectives. Emphasize that scaling Scrum is not just about adding more teams. It requires deliberate decisions about metrics, team topology, and backlog structure. These decisions have direct, measurable impacts on estimation accuracy and delivery predictability. The good news is that the foundational estimation skills from earlier modules still apply at scale. You just need the organizational structures and ceremonies to coordinate them.
