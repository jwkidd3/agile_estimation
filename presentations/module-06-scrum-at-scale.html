Doing Scrum In A Big Way

Scaling Estimation Across Multiple Teams

Agile Project Estimation • 2-Day Workshop

Up to this point, we've discussed estimation within a single team. But most of you deal with multiple teams, and your stakeholders still want a single delivery date. Adding teams makes forecasts less certain, not more, until coordination is right. This module covers how to get that coordination right. We will examine team metrics at scale, how to determine the right number of teams, and the critical question of backlog architecture. These decisions fundamentally shape how estimation works across your organization.

Module Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Identify and track team-level metrics that support healthy estimation at scale
  • Explain why cross-team velocity comparison is misleading and what to use instead
  • Evaluate scaling frameworks (SAFe, LeSS, Nexus) and their impact on estimation
  • Apply Brooks' Law and team topology principles to team sizing decisions
  • Design a backlog architecture that supports multi-team estimation and delivery
  • Describe integration ceremonies that keep scaled teams aligned
Walk through each objective. Emphasize that this module bridges the single-team estimation concepts from earlier modules into multi-team reality. Most organizations will encounter scaling challenges once a product grows beyond what a single Scrum team can handle. The estimation practices we have covered so far still apply, but they need to be adapted and coordinated. This module gives participants the mental models to make those adaptations effectively.

📊 What Are the Team Metrics?

Team-Level Velocity Tracking

Velocity Fundamentals

  • Definition: Story points completed per sprint by a single team
  • Measurement window: Track over 3-5 sprints to establish a reliable range
  • Use it for: Sprint planning capacity and release forecasting
  • Not for: Performance evaluation or cross-team comparison

Healthy velocity pattern:

A stabilized team shows a velocity range, not a fixed number. For example: 28-35 points per sprint. Use the average for planning, but acknowledge the range for risk management.

Watch out for: Velocity used as a target or KPI. Teams will inflate estimates to "hit the number," destroying the metric's usefulness.

Velocity is the most commonly tracked Agile metric, and unfortunately the most commonly misused. Emphasize that velocity is a planning tool, not a performance tool. The moment you start rewarding teams for higher velocity, they will simply inflate their story point estimates. A team that estimates a task at 5 points one sprint will start calling it an 8 the next. The number goes up, but no more work gets done. Velocity only works as a planning input when teams have the psychological safety to estimate honestly. Discuss the concept of a velocity range rather than a single number, and how the range itself is informative about team predictability.

📊 What Are the Team Metrics?

Cross-Team Velocity & Aggregation

Metric Team Alpha Team Beta
Velocity 45 pts/sprint 28 pts/sprint
Team Size 8 people 5 people
Domain Frontend UI Backend infra

Why Cross-Team Comparison Misleads: Story points are a team-internal measure. Comparing Team Alpha's 45 to Team Beta's 28 is like comparing kilometers to miles — the units look similar but measure differently. If your organization already compares team velocities, suggest story count or throughput (stories completed per sprint) as a more apples-to-apples alternative.

⚠️ Normalized Story Points

  • Attempt to create a "universal" point scale across teams
  • Dangers: False precision, encourages comparison, teams lose estimation ownership
  • SAFe note: SAFe recommends normalized story points for portfolio-level planning. If your organization uses SAFe, follow the framework's guidance — but be aware of the trade-offs.

✅ Better Alternatives

  • Aggregate velocity: Sum of all team velocities for rough program-level forecasting — use as a trend, not an absolute
  • Story count: Less susceptible to calibration differences across teams
This slide combines two critical concepts. First, cross-team velocity comparison: Team Alpha has 45 velocity and Team Beta has 28, but that does not mean Alpha is more productive. Different team sizes, different domains, different calibrations. When managers compare velocities across teams, it creates perverse incentives and destroys trust. Second, how to aggregate for program-level planning. Normalized story points are tempting but fragile, requiring constant recalibration and removing team autonomy. Better alternatives include aggregate velocity used as a trend line, and counting stories rather than points, which sidesteps the calibration problem entirely as long as teams split stories to roughly similar sizes.

📊 What Are the Team Metrics?

Team Health Metrics & the Scrum of Scrums

🎯

Predictability

Planned vs. delivered story points. Target: 80-100% consistently.

😊

Team Happiness

Regular team morale surveys. Trend matters more than score.

🐛

Quality (Defect Rate)

Escaped defects per sprint. Correlates with sustainable pace.

⏱️

Cycle Time

Start to done per story. Identifies bottlenecks.

🔄

Sprint Stability

Scope changes during sprint. Track the delivery cost of mid-sprint scope changes.

🤝

Dependency Health

Cross-team blockers resolved on time. Critical at scale.

The Scrum of Scrums: A coordination ceremony where one representative from each team shares: (1) What did we accomplish? (2) What are we doing next? (3) What impediments affect other teams? This is where dependency health metrics become visible at the program level.

Beyond velocity, healthy teams track a constellation of metrics that paint a fuller picture. Predictability tells you how well the team estimates and plans. Team happiness is a leading indicator of future productivity problems. Quality measured through escaped defects reveals whether teams are cutting corners to maintain velocity. Cycle time is arguably more actionable than velocity because it shows where work gets stuck. Sprint stability measures mid-sprint scope changes, and at scale, dependency health becomes critical because blocked teams cascade delays. The Scrum of Scrums ceremony is where these metrics surface at the program level. Emphasize that the Scrum of Scrums is not a status meeting. It is a coordination and impediment-removal session focused on cross-team dependencies. In many organizations, mid-sprint changes come from above, not from poor planning. The value of tracking is giving teams data to show stakeholders the cost: 'Every mid-sprint scope change costs us roughly 2-3 story points of capacity, which pushes the delivery date out by X days.'

🏢 How Many Teams?

Scaling Frameworks Overview

🚀 SAFe

Scaled Agile Framework

  • 5-12 teams per Agile Release Train (ART)
  • PI Planning every 8-12 weeks
  • Prescriptive roles & ceremonies
50-125 people per ART

🌱 LeSS

Large-Scale Scrum

  • 2-8 teams sharing one product backlog
  • One Product Owner, minimal extra roles
Up to ~60 people

🔗 Nexus

Scaled Professional Scrum

  • 3-9 teams building one product
  • Nexus Integration Team coordinates
  • Focus on reducing dependencies
Up to ~70 people

Key insight: The framework you choose directly impacts how estimation works at scale. SAFe normalizes estimation across teams; LeSS keeps team-level estimation intact; Nexus focuses on integration risk.

Give a high-level overview of each framework without going too deep, since this is an estimation course, not a scaling frameworks course. The key point is that each framework makes different trade-offs that affect estimation. SAFe provides the most structure and explicitly addresses estimation through PI Planning, where teams collectively estimate and align on PI objectives. LeSS takes a minimalist approach, keeping Scrum mostly unchanged and letting teams self-organize around a shared backlog. Nexus is somewhere in between, with a dedicated integration team that helps coordinate. Ask the class which frameworks they have encountered. Many organizations implement a hybrid or customized version.

🏢 How Many Teams?

Feature Teams vs. Component Teams

👥 Feature Teams

  • Cross-functional: can deliver end-to-end features
  • Aligned to customer value streams
  • Estimation benefit: Each team estimates and delivers whole stories independently
  • Challenge: Requires broad skill sets; knowledge duplication across teams
vs

⚙️ Component Teams

  • Specialized: owns a specific layer or service
  • Aligned to architecture (frontend, backend, DB)
  • Estimation challenge: A single feature requires estimates from multiple teams
  • Challenge: High dependency overhead; integration risk

Impact on estimation: Component teams turn every feature into a multi-team coordination problem. A story that one feature team can estimate as "8 points" becomes three separate stories across three component teams, each needing coordination. The total effort is almost always greater than the sum of parts due to integration and communication overhead.

A note on the lab: Component teams are sometimes the right choice — especially when deep specialization is required or when the architecture genuinely demands it. The lab assigns component teams deliberately — you will experience the dependency overhead this creates.

This is a critical architectural decision that directly impacts estimation complexity. Feature teams are generally preferred in modern Agile practice because they reduce dependencies. When a feature team estimates a user story, they are estimating the whole thing. With component teams, a single user story might require work from the frontend team, the API team, and the database team. Each team estimates their portion, but nobody estimates the integration work. The coordination cost is often invisible in the estimates but very real in execution. Ask the class: if you have three component teams and each estimates their portion of a feature at 5 points, is the total effort 15 points? Almost never. The actual effort is typically 20-25 points once you account for handoffs, integration testing, and communication overhead. Acknowledge that component teams are sometimes the right choice, but the lab intentionally uses them so students experience the coordination overhead firsthand.

🏢 How Many Teams?

Brooks' Law and Team Size

📐 Brooks' Law

"Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later."

Communication channels: C = n(n - 1) / 2

5 people = 10 channels • 7 = 21 • 9 = 36 • 12 = 66 • 15 = 105

👥 Optimal Team Size: 5-9 people

The Scrum Guide recommends 10 or fewer. Research shows 5-9 as the sweet spot.

Dunbar's Numbers

  • ~5 — Close collaborators (one Scrum team core)
  • ~15 — Trusted group (extended team + stakeholders)
  • ~50 — Tribe / ART scale
  • ~150 — Maximum meaningful relationships
Brooks' Law is from Fred Brooks' 1975 book The Mythical Man-Month. The communication formula shows exponential growth. Going from 5 to 9 people nearly quadruples the communication channels, which is why estimation accuracy tends to decrease in larger teams. Dunbar's number from anthropological research maps well to scaling framework limits. SAFe ARTs cap at about 125 people. The Scrum Guide explicitly recommends teams of 10 or fewer, and most practitioners find the 5-9 range to be the sweet spot for balancing throughput with communication overhead.

🏢 How Many Teams?

When to Split or Grow Teams

✂️ Signals to Split a Team

  • Team exceeds 9 members
  • Sprint planning takes too long due to scope breadth
  • Velocity stable but backlog growth outpaces delivery
  • Distinct sub-domains emerging within the team's work

📈 Signals to Grow (Not Split)

  • Team lacks a critical skill (e.g., no QA, no UX)
  • Team is under 5 members and struggling with absences
  • Work is tightly coupled; splitting would create excessive dependencies

Estimation impact of splitting: When you split a team, expect velocity to decrease for 3-5 sprints as teams re-form, re-calibrate estimation baselines, and establish new norms. Plan for this dip.

The decision to split or grow a team has real estimation consequences. When you split, you reset the team's estimation history. Each new team needs to recalibrate, and for several sprints, total output will actually decrease. This is Tuckman's model in action. Adding people to an existing team also temporarily slows it down. The key question: is the constraint skill gaps (grow) or scope breadth (split)?

📋 How Many Product Backlogs?

Backlog Architecture: Single vs. Multi-Level

📖 Single Backlog (LeSS)

  • One product = one backlog = one Product Owner
  • Teams pull from the same prioritized backlog during sprint planning
  • Each team estimates only items they select, using their own scales
  • Advantage: Maximum flexibility — highest-value work always gets done first
  • Challenge: PO becomes a bottleneck beyond ~4 teams

🎯 Multi-Level Backlogs (SAFe)

Portfolio
Epics
➡️
Program
Features
➡️
Team
Stories
  • Features estimated in normalized points or T-shirt sizes at program level
  • PI Planning: teams decompose features into stories and estimate capacity
  • Team velocity feeds into program-level capacity planning
  • Trade-off: More ceremony, but portfolio-level visibility across dozens of teams

Choosing your model: Single backlog maximizes flexibility and suits teams with broad skill sets and low inter-team dependencies. Multi-level backlogs provide strategic alignment and suit organizations that need to coordinate across many teams and report progress at multiple levels. Estimation happens at multiple levels in SAFe: T-shirt sizes for epics, normalized points for features, and team-specific points for stories.

The single backlog model is what LeSS advocates. One Product Owner maintains one ordered backlog. During sprint planning, all teams gather and select work from the top. This maximizes value delivery. From an estimation perspective it is clean: each team estimates the items they select using their own calibration. The main challenge is the PO bottleneck beyond 4-6 teams. SAFe takes the opposite approach with a backlog hierarchy. Each level has its own estimation approach. PI Planning is the keystone ceremony where all of this comes together. Teams break features into stories, estimate them, identify dependencies, and align on PI objectives. The overhead is real but for large organizations the alignment can be worth it. Ask participants about their experience with either model.

📋 How Many Product Backlogs?

Dependencies & Integration Ceremonies

🔗 Dependency Types

  • Knowledge: "We need Team B's expertise to estimate this"
  • Task: "We cannot start until Team B finishes their API"
  • Resource: "We share a DBA with two other teams"

The dependency tax: Cross-team dependencies typically add significant overhead — teams commonly report 15-30% in coordination costs. Use a dependency board to visualize cross-team blockers and assign buffer stories in case dependent work is delayed.

Ceremony Frequency Estimation Impact
PI Planning Every 8-12 wks Teams estimate features, align on PI objectives
Scrum of Scrums 2-3x per week Early warning on at-risk dependencies
Cross-Team Refinement Weekly Reduces estimation variance across teams
System Demo End of sprint Reveals gaps missed in team estimates
Overall Retro End of PI Calibrate accuracy, refine buffers

Rule of thumb: The number of integration ceremonies scales with the number of dependencies between teams, not the number of teams. Two tightly coupled teams may need more coordination than five loosely coupled teams.

Dependencies are the silent killer of estimation accuracy at scale. When teams estimate in isolation, they rarely account for the overhead of coordinating with other teams. A story that seems like a 5-pointer when estimated by one team becomes an 8 once you factor in waiting for another team's API, attending cross-team design sessions, and integration testing. Platform teams present a unique challenge since their work enables but does not directly deliver business features; reserve a percentage of their capacity for supporting feature teams. For ceremonies: PI Planning is where all teams in an ART come together to plan the next Program Increment. Cross-team refinement is often overlooked but critical for ensuring compatible estimates. The System Demo at the end of each sprint is where hidden integration costs become visible. Making dependencies visible is the first step to managing them.

📋 How Many Product Backlogs?

Aggregating Multi-Team Forecasts

Worked Example: 3 teams with velocities 26, 30, 22. Total backlog ~200 points. But dependency chains mean work must be sequenced: Gamma builds the data foundations first, Beta builds the APIs next, Alpha integrates last.

⚠️ Raw Aggregate (Misleading)

78 pts/sprint × 8 sprints = 624 pts capacity

This assumes all teams work in parallel with no dependencies. In reality, dependency chains force sequential work — teams are often waiting, not working.

✅ Critical Path Approach

The longest dependency chain determines the minimum timeline, not total capacity.

If the critical path has 90 points and the constrained team's velocity is 26 pts/sprint, the minimum is ~3.5 sprints for that chain alone — regardless of what other teams can do in parallel.

Process: Identify critical path → Sum points on critical path → Divide by the constrained team's velocity → Add dependency buffer (15-30%) → Compare against deadline

This is exactly the calculation students will do in the lab against a 16-week conference deadline. Walk through the example carefully. The raw aggregate of 78 points per sprint times 8 sprints equals 624 points of theoretical capacity. But if Gamma must finish the data layer before Beta can build APIs, and Beta must finish APIs before Alpha can integrate, then the critical path determines the timeline, not the aggregate capacity. The critical path calculation is: identify the longest chain of dependent work, sum the points on that chain, divide by the velocity of the team that is the bottleneck on that chain, and add a dependency buffer. This is a fundamentally different way of thinking about multi-team forecasting than simply adding up velocities.
🧪 Hands-On Lab

Lab 6: Scaling Estimation Simulation

Apply multi-team estimation in a realistic scaling scenario

  • Split into 3 teams representing Frontend, Backend, and Platform
  • Estimate a shared 8-feature product backlog independently
  • Map cross-team dependencies and determine sequencing — focus on ordering, not sprint-by-sprint assignment
  • Identify bottleneck teams and discuss capacity trade-offs
  • Debate backlog organization strategy (shared vs. per-team vs. program-level)
  • Calculate a delivery date range for all features using aggregate velocity and dependency overhead
  • Work against a fixed 16-week conference deadline — determine what scope fits

Duration: 45 minutes (30 min work + 15 min presentations)  |  Format: 3 teams (full class simulation)

This lab is the centerpiece of the module. Break participants into 3 teams. Give each team a set of feature cards from a shared product backlog. The features should have built-in dependencies between them. Set up the scenario: The VP has committed to a healthcare conference demo in 16 weeks. This is a hard external deadline. Let participants feel the pressure of a real constraint. Have teams estimate independently first, then come together for a mini PI Planning session. They need to map dependencies on a shared program board using string or tape to connect dependent features. Then they calculate aggregate velocity and try to forecast when the full set of features will be delivered. The debrief is the most important part. Ask: Did any team discover a dependency during planning that changed their estimate? Did any feature take longer to estimate because it spanned teams? How confident are you in the aggregate forecast versus your individual team forecasts? This lab makes the abstract concepts of scaling estimation very concrete.

💡 Key Takeaways

📊 Velocity is team-specific

Comparing raw velocity numbers across teams is misleading — each team calibrates differently. Compare trends, not absolute numbers. Use velocity for planning within a team, not for performance evaluation.

👥 Team health drives estimation quality

Track predictability, happiness, quality, and cycle time alongside velocity. Healthy teams produce reliable estimates; stressed teams produce optimistic ones.

🏢 Communication overhead is real

Brooks' Law and Dunbar's Number set hard limits on team size. Keep teams at 5-9 people. Expect a velocity dip when splitting teams.

🔗 Dependencies are the hidden cost

Cross-team dependencies typically add significant overhead — teams commonly report 15-30% in coordination costs. Make dependencies visible. Choose feature teams over component teams when possible.

📋 Backlog architecture shapes estimation

One backlog (LeSS) maximizes flexibility. Multiple backlogs (SAFe) enable portfolio visibility. Choose based on organizational needs and team maturity.

📅 Integration ceremonies prevent surprise delays

PI Planning, Scrum of Scrums, and cross-team refinement keep multi-team estimates aligned with reality. Even lightweight versions — a weekly 15-minute dependency check across team leads — dramatically reduce the integration surprises that blow up timelines.

Review each takeaway and tie it back to the course objectives. Emphasize that scaling Scrum is not just about adding more teams. It requires deliberate decisions about metrics, team topology, and backlog structure. These decisions have direct, measurable impacts on estimation accuracy and delivery predictability. The good news is that the foundational estimation skills from earlier modules still apply at scale. You just need the organizational structures and ceremonies to coordinate them.
