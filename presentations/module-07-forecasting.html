🔮 The Cold Start Challenge

⚠️ Why It's Hard

  • No velocity baseline to project from
  • Unknown team dynamics and capacity
  • Unfamiliar technology or domain
  • Incomplete or evolving requirements
  • Stakeholder pressure for single-point date answers

✅ Available Strategies

  • Reference projects — find analogous work
  • Expert judgment — leverage experience
  • Delphi technique — structured consensus
  • Thin-slicing — run sprints first
  • Monte Carlo — probabilistic simulation
Key Principle: The less data you have, the wider your confidence interval should be. When providing a date, anchor on the pessimistic end and explicitly state the assumptions.
Walk through both columns. The left side validates that this IS genuinely difficult. The pressure for a single date is real — acknowledge it. The goal isn't to refuse the request, it's to give a more useful answer. A range with confidence levels gives decision-makers more to work with than a single date that's probably wrong. Frame it as: 'I can give you a date — let me also give you the confidence level that goes with it.' The right side gives practical approaches we'll explore in detail. Emphasize that the most useful early answer is a date paired with a confidence level and stated assumptions.

🔮 Cold Start Strategies: Reference Projects, Delphi & Thin-Slicing

📋 Reference Projects

  1. Identify 2-3 similar past projects
  2. Gather actual duration and team size data
  3. Adjust for known differences
  4. Create a range: shortest to longest reference
Worked Example: Reference team of 7 averaged 24 pts/sprint → for your team of 6: (24÷7)×6 ≈ 21 pts/sprint. First 3 sprints at ~60% while the team gels ≈ 13 pts/sprint.

🧑‍💼 Delphi Technique

  1. 3-5 experts provide independent blind estimates
  2. Share anonymously, discuss outliers, re-estimate until convergence

Reference projects are your best friend when you have no team-specific data. Walk through the worked example on the slide: if a reference team of 7 averaged 24 points/sprint, scale proportionally to your team of 6 — that's (24/7)×6 ≈ 21 points/sprint. Then apply a ramp-up discount: new teams typically run at about 60% of steady-state velocity for the first 3 sprints while they gel, so plan for roughly 13 pts/sprint initially. Students will use exactly this technique in Lab 7 Step 1. The Delphi technique reduces anchoring bias through anonymity — similar to Planning Poker at the story level. Thin-slicing is the most practical approach: if you can negotiate a 2-sprint ramp-up before locking a forecast, do it. If you can't wait, use reference projects and Delphi to give an honest initial range, label it as preliminary, and lock in a re-forecast date after 2 sprints. Monte Carlo simulations give probabilistic forecasts — even with 3-4 sprints of data, you can run meaningful simulations. The cost of running 2 sprints before forecasting is far less than forecasting on the wrong date.

📈 Velocity-Based Forecasting

📊 Using Velocity for Forecasts

  • Use rolling average of last 3-5 sprints
  • Track best, average, and worst rolling average
  • Calculate range: optimistic → pessimistic
  • Account for known disruptions (holidays, onboarding)
Never use:
  • "Best sprint ever" as the forecast
  • Velocity from first 1-2 sprints only
  • Velocity from a different team composition

Velocity-based forecasting is the bread and butter of Agile forecasting. The rolling average smooths out sprint-to-sprint variation. Using the best/average/worst rolling averages from recent history gives a natural range. Emphasize that you should never cherry-pick your best sprint — that's a recipe for broken forecasts. Also, if team composition changes significantly, your historical velocity may no longer be valid.

📈 Release Burndown & Burnup Charts

📉 Burndown Chart

  • Shows remaining work over time
  • Ideal line from total scope to zero
  • Actual line reveals velocity trends
  • Limitation Hides scope changes

Useful when: Scope is fixed and well-understood

📈 Burnup Chart

  • Shows work completed AND total scope
  • Two lines: completed work + scope line
  • Reveals scope creep visually
  • Advantage Transparent scope changes

Useful when: Scope may evolve — most real projects

Best Practice: Use burnup charts for stakeholder communication. The gap between the two lines makes the conversation about scope vs. timeline visible and honest.
Compare burndown and burnup charts. Burndowns are simpler but hide scope changes — if you add 20 story points of new scope, the burndown line jumps UP, which can be confusing. Burnup charts make this transparent: the completed work line keeps rising, and the scope line also rises when new work is added. The visual gap between them is what's left to do. Most experienced Agile coaches prefer burnup charts for release-level tracking.

⏳ When Historical Data Becomes Stale

  • Team changes — >30% turnover invalidates velocity
  • Technology shifts — new platform or architecture
  • Process changes — new DoD, sprint length change
  • Time decay — data older than 6-8 sprints loses relevance
Stakeholder message template: "Based on our last 6 sprints, we forecast completion in 5 to 7 sprints (10-14 weeks). We have moderate confidence in the 5-sprint target and high confidence in the 7-sprint target."
Confidence intervals are crucial for honest communication. Notice how the range widens as confidence increases — this is counterintuitive for some stakeholders. Help them understand that a narrow range means lower confidence. For contractual commitments, use the 85% or 95% interval. Also discuss when data goes stale — if more than 30% of the team has changed, treat it as a cold start. The stakeholder message template gives participants a script they can use — always give a range and associate confidence levels with it.

🛡️ Buffer Strategies: Types, Sizing & Trade-Offs

⏰ Time Buffers

  • Add extra sprints beyond the forecast
  • Typical: 15-30% of estimated duration
  • Placed at end of release, not per sprint

📦 Feature Buffers (Scope Buffers)

  • Prioritize into must-have / should-have / defer to next release
  • If ahead: pull in should-have and deferred features
  • If behind: flex scope on should-haves first, then defer

✅ Buffers Help When...

  • Requirements are genuinely uncertain
  • External dependencies are outside your control
  • Team is new and velocity is still stabilizing
  • Buffer consumption is tracked and visible

❌ Buffers Mask Problems When...

  • Team consistently uses 100% of buffers
  • Buffers hide poor estimation discipline
  • Scope creep is accepted without discussion
Critical Chain Approach: Remove individual task padding, aggregate it into a single project buffer at the end. This prevents Parkinson's Law (work expands to fill available time). Feature buffers are often the more Agile-friendly choice — they preserve the delivery date while allowing scope flexibility.
Explain the two main types of buffers. Time buffers add sprints; feature buffers add negotiable scope. Feature buffers are often more Agile-friendly because they preserve the delivery date while allowing scope flexibility. The Critical Chain approach pools all uncertainty into a single buffer, which is statistically more efficient. Buffers are a tool, not a crutch. If you always consume 100% of your buffer, you don't have a buffer — you have an estimation bias. Track buffer consumption as a separate metric.

📊 Metrics: Leading vs. Lagging & Measurement Cadence

➡️ Leading Indicators (Predictive)

  • Sprint backlog readiness — stories refined and estimated?
  • WIP count — too many items in progress signals problems
  • Blocked item count — impediments predict velocity drops
  • Team confidence vote — how the team feels about the sprint goal

⬅️ Lagging Indicators (Reflective)

  • Velocity — what the team actually delivered
  • Sprint goal success rate — met or missed?
  • Defect count — quality of delivered work
  • Cycle time — how long items took to complete

📅 When to Measure What

CadenceMetrics
DailyWIP, blocked items, burndown
Per SprintVelocity, defects, cycle time
Per ReleaseBurnup, scope change %
QuarterlyVelocity trends, forecast accuracy
Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Use leading indicators to steer during a sprint; lagging indicators to learn after a sprint. Both feed into better forecasts.
Leading indicators are like the windshield of a car — they help you see what's coming. Lagging indicators are the rearview mirror — they show where you've been. You need both for good forecasting. Many teams only track lagging indicators and are surprised by problems. For the cadence table: daily metrics should be lightweight standup items, sprint metrics feed retrospectives, release metrics are for stakeholders, and quarterly is strategic. If you can't act on a metric at a given cadence, don't track it at that cadence. Velocity should never be a performance metric — the moment you reward higher velocity, you get point inflation.

🔧 Refactoring: Capacity Allocation & ROI

📊 Capacity Allocation Models

  • Fixed percentage: Reserve 15-20% of each sprint
  • Boy Scout Rule: Leave code better than you found it — baked into every story
  • Tech debt backlog: Prioritize refactoring items alongside features

⚠️ Anti-Pattern

"We'll refactor after the release." This almost never happens. Technical debt must be addressed continuously, not deferred indefinitely.

💰 ROI: 12-Sprint Comparison

  • Without Refactoring: Velocity 26 → 22 → 18 = 264 pts
  • With 15% Refactoring: Velocity 22 → 23 → 24 = 276 pts

✅ Recommended Approach

Combine Boy Scout Rule (ongoing) with a 15% capacity reserve (planned). The refactoring team delivers more total value over a release cycle with more predictable forecasts.

The Crossover Point: Refactoring produces lower initial velocity but higher sustained velocity. Make refactoring visible by creating backlog stories with clear acceptance criteria.
Walk through each allocation model. The fixed percentage approach is the most sustainable. The Boy Scout Rule is great for small improvements but doesn't address large-scale refactoring needs. The recommended combination gives you both. The ROI comparison shows the numbers side by side: the team that invests 15% in refactoring maintains stable velocity and actually delivers more points over 12 sprints. When refactoring is invisible, stakeholders think the team is slow. When it's visible in the backlog, they understand where the investment goes and why.

🧪 TDD & ATTD: Metrics and Forecasting Impact

🔧 TDD Metrics

Write test first → Write code → Refactor

  • Code coverage trend — stable at 70-90%
  • Test execution time — fast feedback loop
  • Defects in TDD'd vs. non-TDD'd code

✅ ATTD Metrics

  • Acceptance test pass rate at sprint end
  • Stories reopened rate — should decrease with ATTD
  • Stakeholder acceptance rate in first review

MetricWithoutWith TDD/ATTD

The Virtuous Cycle: Better tests → fewer defects → less rework → stable velocity → accurate forecasts → stakeholder trust. Velocity variance drops 2-3x with mature TDD/ATTD, enabling dramatically narrower forecast intervals.
Walk through each metric category. For TDD, coverage alone doesn't tell you much — you need to look at the trend and combine it with defect data. For ATTD, the stories-reopened rate is a killer metric. The velocity variance row in the table is the most important for forecasting: reducing variance from plus/minus 50% to plus/minus 20% means your "most likely" forecast is dramatically more reliable. Don't mandate coverage targets — that leads to gaming behavior. Instead, track trends and the correlation with defect data.

📅 Date-Driven Forecasting

"The release date is June 30. How much scope can we deliver?"

Deliverable Scope Available Sprints × Avg Velocity = Max Story Points

Use when the date is fixed (regulatory, contractual, market window)

📦 Scope-Driven Forecasting

"We need all 200 story points. When will we be done?"

Delivery Date Total Story Points ÷ Avg Velocity = Required Sprints

Use when scope is non-negotiable (MVP, compliance requirements)

When both are fixed: If the date AND scope are non-negotiable, the only levers are resources and quality. Use the formulas above to show the gap: "We can deliver X of Y points by the date. To close the gap, we need [more capacity / reduced scope / adjusted quality bar]." Make the constraint triangle visible.
These are the two fundamental forecasting modes. In practice, most organizations work date-driven — someone upstream has set a date and your job is to figure out what scope fits. Spend more time on the left card. WORKED EXAMPLE for date-driven (this mirrors what students will do in Lab 7): "The launch date is 20 weeks out. That gives us 10 two-week sprints. Our last 4 sprints averaged about 24 points. So our max delivery capacity is roughly 240 points. But we've been getting about 5 points of scope added per sprint — that's 50 points of scope growth over 10 sprints. So net delivery is closer to 190 points. Our remaining backlog is 183 points. On paper it fits — but only if scope growth holds steady and velocity doesn't dip. That's a 50% confidence forecast at best. For 85% confidence, plan for the must-have features only and treat the rest as stretch goals." The key insight: when the date is fixed, the conversation shifts from "when will we be done?" to "what can we deliver by this date?" — that's a much more productive conversation because it gives stakeholders a clear scope trade-off instead of a vague date range. Emphasize the "When both are fixed" box. This is the reality many teams face. The formulas make the gap visible and turn an emotional negotiation into a data-backed conversation. In the lab, students will practice exactly this: given a fixed conference date, work backward to determine what fits and what gets cut. When walking students through the backward calculation, demonstrate the scope triage: categorize remaining features into must-have (demo-critical), should-have (include if velocity allows), and defer (post-launch). This is the exercise they'll practice in Lab 7 Step 3.

📋 Capacity Planning & Communicating Forecasts

📊 Capacity Adjustments

Adjust velocity per sprint for:

  • Team absences (leave, training, onboarding)
  • Holidays and company events
  • Support rotation or on-call duties
  • Always reserve slack for unknowns — planning to 100% capacity guarantees failure

❌ Avoid (Erodes Trust Over Time)

  • "It will be done by April 15." (single point, no confidence level)
  • "We need exactly 6 sprints." (false precision)
  • "We promise to deliver all 40 stories." (scope + date both locked)

✅ Do Say

  • "We forecast completion between April 1-30, with highest confidence in the April 15-22 range."
  • "Based on current velocity, we need 5-7 sprints."
  • "Budget range: $220K-$280K based on scope."
  • "We forecast the top 30 stories with high confidence, and target all 40 as a stretch."
Template: "Based on [data source], we forecast [scope] delivered in [range]. Confidence: [level]. Key risks: [risks]. Next update: [cadence]."
Capacity planning is where the rubber meets the road. A common mistake is using the same velocity for every sprint without adjusting for known capacity changes. The "Don't Say / Do Say" comparison shows the shift from single-point certainty (which stakeholders want) to ranges with confidence levels (which builds trust). The template at the bottom gives participants a script for their next stakeholder meeting. This communication style builds trust over time because you're honest about uncertainty rather than promising and missing.

🔄 Continuous Refinement

  • After each sprint, replace estimates with actuals
  • Track estimate vs. actual — apply correction factors
  • Forecast ranges narrow as data replaces assumptions

📊 Throughput-Based Forecasting

Instead of estimating story points, count stories completed per sprint.

  • Throughput: 8-12 stories/sprint
  • Remaining: 45 stories
  • Forecast: 4-6 sprints
Works when: stories are consistently similar in size, team has stable throughput, and estimation provides diminishing returns.
Keep estimating when: story sizes vary dramatically, team is new, stakeholders require estimate-based forecasts, or portfolio planning needs point-based allocation.
Walk through the maturity journey from pure estimation to throughput-based forecasting. Larger stories are consistently underestimated — a 13-point story is typically underestimated by 50%, which is a powerful argument for story splitting. The #NoEstimates movement argues that for mature teams with well-refined backlogs, counting items is simpler and equally accurate. But new teams benefit enormously from the estimation conversation. The answer is: use the right approach for your context. Most teams start with estimates and some evolve to throughput-based forecasting.
