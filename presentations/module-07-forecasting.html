🔮 Forecasting Without Any History

Starting from zero — new teams, new products, new domains

This is one of the hardest situations in project management. A stakeholder asks "when will it be done?" and you have no velocity data, no past projects to reference, and possibly a brand new team. Let's explore practical strategies for this scenario.

🔮 The Cold Start Challenge

⚠️ Why It's Hard

  • No velocity baseline to project from
  • Unknown team dynamics and capacity
  • Unfamiliar technology or domain
  • Incomplete or evolving requirements
  • Pressure to commit to dates immediately

✅ Available Strategies

  • Reference projects — find analogous work
  • Expert judgment — leverage experience
  • Delphi technique — structured consensus
  • Thin-slicing — run sprints first
  • Monte Carlo — probabilistic simulation
Key Principle: The less data you have, the wider your confidence interval must be. Never give a single-point estimate when you have no history.
Walk through both columns. The left side validates that this IS genuinely difficult — don't let anyone pressure you into false precision. The right side gives practical approaches we'll explore in detail. Emphasize that the honest answer early on is a range, not a date.

🔮 Reference Projects & Expert Judgment

📋 Reference Project Approach

  1. Identify 2-3 similar past projects (size, domain, complexity)
  2. Gather actual duration and team size data
  3. Adjust for known differences (technology, team experience)
  4. Create a range: shortest reference to longest reference

🧑‍💼 Delphi Technique

  1. Assemble 3-5 experienced practitioners
  2. Each provides independent estimate (blind)
  3. Share results anonymously, discuss outliers
  4. Re-estimate until convergence occurs
Pro Tip: The Delphi technique works because it reduces anchoring bias. The anonymity prevents the loudest voice from dominating the estimate, similar to how Planning Poker works at the story level.
Reference projects are your best friend when you have no team-specific data. Even if the projects aren't a perfect match, they provide a grounding point. The Delphi technique scales this up by leveraging multiple experts. Remind participants that Planning Poker, which they learned in the relative sizing module, is essentially Delphi applied at the story level.

🎲 Monte Carlo Simulation Concepts

  • Run thousands of simulations using random sampling of historical throughput
  • Each simulation produces a different completion date
  • Aggregate results into a probability distribution
ConfidenceCompletion
50%Sprint 8
70%Sprint 9

Thin-slicing is the most practical approach for most teams. The idea is simple: don't commit to a delivery date until you have at least 1-2 sprints of actual data. This is a negotiation point with stakeholders — explain that the cost of running 2 sprints before committing is far less than the cost of committing to the wrong date. Monte Carlo simulations are powerful because they give probabilistic forecasts rather than single-point estimates. Even with just 3-4 sprints of data, you can run meaningful simulations.

📈 Forecasting Using Historical Data

Leveraging velocity trends, burndowns, and statistical confidence

Now let's look at the much better scenario: when you HAVE historical data to work with. This is where forecasting becomes truly powerful. We'll explore velocity-based forecasting, burndown/burnup charts, and how to express forecasts as confidence intervals.

📈 Velocity-Based Forecasting

📊 Using Velocity for Forecasts

  • Use rolling average of last 3-5 sprints
  • Track best, average, and worst velocity
  • Calculate range: optimistic → pessimistic
  • Account for known disruptions (holidays, onboarding)
Never use:
  • "Best sprint ever" as the forecast
  • Velocity from first 1-2 sprints only
  • Velocity from a different team composition

With 80 points remaining Optimistic: 80 ÷ 28 = 3 sprints
Likely: 80 ÷ 25.7 = ~3.1 sprints
Pessimistic: 80 ÷ 22 = ~3.6 sprints
Velocity-based forecasting is the bread and butter of Agile forecasting. The rolling average smooths out sprint-to-sprint variation. Using the best/average/worst from recent history gives a natural range. Emphasize that you should never cherry-pick your best sprint — that's a recipe for broken commitments. Also, if team composition changes significantly, your historical velocity may no longer be valid.

📈 Release Burndown & Burnup Charts

📉 Burndown Chart

  • Shows remaining work over time
  • Ideal line from total scope to zero
  • Actual line reveals velocity trends
  • Limitation Hides scope changes

Useful when: Scope is fixed and well-understood

📈 Burnup Chart

  • Shows work completed AND total scope
  • Two lines: completed work + scope line
  • Reveals scope creep visually
  • Advantage Transparent scope changes

Useful when: Scope may evolve — most real projects

Best Practice: Use burnup charts for stakeholder communication. The gap between the two lines makes the conversation about scope vs. timeline visible and honest.
Compare burndown and burnup charts. Burndowns are simpler but hide scope changes — if you add 20 story points of new scope, the burndown line jumps UP, which can be confusing. Burnup charts make this transparent: the completed work line keeps rising, and the scope line also rises when new work is added. The visual gap between them is what's left to do. Most experienced Agile coaches prefer burnup charts for release-level tracking.

⏳ When Historical Data Becomes Stale

  • Team changes — >30% turnover invalidates velocity
  • Technology shifts — new platform or architecture
  • Process changes — new DoD, sprint length change
  • Time decay — data older than 6-8 sprints loses relevance
Stakeholder message template: "Based on our last 6 sprints, we forecast completion in 5 to 7 sprints (10-14 weeks). We have moderate confidence in the 5-sprint target and high confidence in the 7-sprint target."
Confidence intervals are crucial for honest communication. Notice how the range widens as confidence increases — this is counterintuitive for some stakeholders. Help them understand that a narrow range means lower confidence. For contractual commitments, use the 85% or 95% interval. Also discuss when data goes stale — teams often keep using velocity data from months ago when the team and context have changed significantly. Rule of thumb: if more than 30% of the team has changed, treat it as a cold start. The stakeholder message template at the bottom gives participants a script they can use — always give a range and associate confidence levels with it.

🛡️ Deciding Whether to Buffer

Schedule protection strategies — when they help and when they hide problems

Buffers are a controversial topic in Agile. Some practitioners say they're essential for realistic planning; others say they mask dysfunction. The truth is nuanced. Let's explore when and how to use them effectively.

🛡️ Buffer Types & Sizing

⏰ Time Buffers (Schedule Buffers)

  • Add extra sprints beyond the forecast
  • Absorb unexpected delays, scope additions
  • Typical: 15-30% of estimated duration
  • Placed at end of release, not per sprint

📦 Feature Buffers (Scope Buffers)

  • Prioritize features into must-have / nice-to-have
  • Commit to must-haves, plan nice-to-haves as buffer
  • If ahead of schedule: deliver bonus features
  • If behind: cut nice-to-haves without missing the date

📐 Buffer Sizing Techniques

MethodBuffer Size
Square Root of Sum of Squares√(Σ variance²)
Percentage-based20-30% of duration
Risk-adjustedBased on risk register
HistoricalBased on past overruns
Critical Chain Approach: Remove individual task padding, aggregate it into a single project buffer at the end. This prevents Parkinson's Law (work expands to fill available time).
Explain the two main types of buffers. Time buffers add sprints; feature buffers add negotiable scope. Feature buffers are often more Agile-friendly because they preserve the delivery date while allowing scope flexibility — which is what Agile is all about. The Critical Chain approach from Eliyahu Goldratt is particularly interesting: instead of each task having its own padding, you pool all the uncertainty into a single buffer at the project level, which is statistically more efficient.

🛡️ When Buffers Help vs. When They Mask Problems

✅ Buffers Help When...

  • Requirements are genuinely uncertain
  • External dependencies are outside your control
  • Team is new and velocity is still stabilizing
  • Buffer consumption is tracked and visible
vs

❌ Buffers Mask Problems When...

  • Team consistently uses 100% of buffers
  • Buffers hide poor estimation discipline
  • Scope creep is accepted without discussion
  • No one asks why buffers are consumed

📋 Feature Buffer Example (MoSCoW)

PriorityFeaturesPointsStatus
Must HaveUser auth, dashboard, core reports85Committed
Should HaveAdvanced filters, export to PDF30Target
Nice to HaveCustom themes, social sharing25Buffer scope
This is the critical distinction. Buffers are a tool, not a crutch. If you always consume 100% of your buffer, you don't have a buffer — you have an estimation bias. Track buffer consumption as a separate metric. Feature buffers are particularly elegant because they align with Agile's core principle of prioritization. The conversation shifts from "we might be late" to "we will definitely deliver the must-haves on time, and we're targeting the should-haves as well." MoSCoW prioritization (Must/Should/Could/Won't) is a formal framework for this. If velocity is higher than expected, deliver bonus scope. If lower, cut Nice to Have and protect the date.

✅ Ensuring Quality

Why quality is an estimation input, not an afterthought

Quality directly impacts forecasting accuracy. Teams that skip quality practices may appear faster in the short term but become slower and less predictable over time. Let's quantify this relationship.

✅ Quality as an Estimation Input

📉 Low Quality Impact

  • Technical debt accumulates, slowing velocity sprint over sprint
  • Defect rates rise, consuming capacity for rework
  • Unpredictable velocity — some sprints are dominated by bug fixing
  • Estimates become unreliable because "done" keeps getting redefined

📈 High Quality Impact

  • Stable velocity — consistent throughput sprint to sprint
  • Low rework — features stay "done"
  • Predictable capacity — team works on planned items, not firefighting
  • Forecasts are trustworthy because delivery is consistent
The Quality-Velocity Relationship Effective Velocity = Measured Velocity − (Defect Fix Rate × Avg Points per Defect)
This slide makes the case that quality is not separate from estimation — it's a core input. A team with 30 story points velocity but spending 8 points on defect fixes has an effective velocity of 22 for new features. If stakeholders are asking "when will feature X ship?" they care about effective velocity, not raw velocity. Track both and make the distinction visible.

📊 Understanding What to Measure and When

Choosing the right metrics at the right cadence

Measurement is critical for forecasting, but measuring the wrong things — or the right things at the wrong time — can be worse than not measuring at all. Let's build a measurement strategy that supports accurate forecasting.

📊 Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

➡️ Leading Indicators (Predictive)

Tell you what's about to happen

  • Sprint backlog readiness — are stories refined and estimated?
  • WIP count — too many items in progress signals problems ahead
  • Blocked item count — impediments predict velocity drops
  • Team confidence vote — how the team feels about the sprint goal
  • Code review queue depth — bottlenecks forming

⬅️ Lagging Indicators (Reflective)

Tell you what already happened

  • Velocity — what the team actually delivered
  • Sprint goal success rate — met or missed?
  • Defect count — quality of delivered work
  • Cycle time — how long items took to complete
  • Forecast accuracy — predicted vs. actual delivery
Balanced Approach: Use leading indicators to steer during a sprint. Use lagging indicators to learn after a sprint. Both feed into better forecasts.
Leading indicators are like the windshield of a car — they help you see what's coming. Lagging indicators are the rearview mirror — they show where you've been. You need both for good forecasting. Many teams only track lagging indicators (velocity, defects) and are surprised by problems. If you also track leading indicators like WIP and blocked items, you can predict velocity problems before they show up in the numbers.

For the cadence table: daily metrics should be lightweight standup items, sprint metrics feed retrospectives, release metrics are for stakeholders, and quarterly is strategic. If you can't act on a metric at a given cadence, don't track it at that cadence.

🔧 Refactoring Formalized and Measured

Making technical improvement visible, planned, and tracked

Refactoring is essential for sustainable velocity, but it's often invisible to stakeholders. If it's invisible, it gets cut when schedules are tight — which is exactly when you need it most. Let's formalize it.

🔧 Allocating Capacity for Refactoring

📊 Capacity Allocation Models

  • Fixed percentage: Reserve 15-20% of each sprint for refactoring and tech debt
  • Dedicated sprints: Every 4th sprint is a "hardening" sprint (less Agile-friendly)
  • Boy Scout Rule: Leave code better than you found it — baked into every story
  • Tech debt backlog: Prioritize refactoring items alongside features using business value

✅ Recommended Approach

Combine Boy Scout Rule (ongoing) with a 15% capacity reserve (planned). This ensures continuous small improvements plus capacity for larger refactoring initiatives.

⚠️ Anti-Pattern

"We'll refactor after the release." This almost never happens. Technical debt must be addressed continuously, not deferred indefinitely.

Walk through each allocation model. The fixed percentage approach is the most sustainable. Dedicated sprints feel good but often get cancelled under schedule pressure. The Boy Scout Rule is great for small improvements but doesn't address large-scale refactoring needs. The recommended combination gives you both. Emphasize the anti-pattern — "we'll refactor later" is the most common lie in software development.

🧪 Measuring TDD and ATTD

How testing practices improve estimation accuracy and predictability

Test-Driven Development and Acceptance Test-Driven Development are powerful quality practices that have a direct, measurable impact on forecasting accuracy. Let's look at the metrics that prove this and how to track them.

🧪 TDD Metrics That Matter

🔧 Test-Driven Development (TDD)

Write test first → Write code → Refactor

  • Code coverage trend — should be stable at 70-90%
  • Test-to-code ratio — lines of test code vs. production code
  • Test execution time — fast feedback enables fast iteration
  • Defects in TDD'd code vs. non-TDD'd code — the comparison that proves the value

✅ Acceptance Test-Driven Development (ATTD)

Define acceptance criteria as executable tests before coding

  • Acceptance test pass rate — % passing at sprint end
  • Stories reopened rate — should decrease with ATTD
  • Acceptance criteria clarity — measured by refinement rework
  • Stakeholder acceptance rate — % accepted in first review
TDD Discipline Indicator TDD Adherence = (Commits with Tests First) ÷ (Total Commits) × 100%
Walk through each metric category. For TDD, code coverage is the most commonly tracked, but coverage alone doesn't tell you much — you need to look at the trend and combine it with defect data. The test-to-code ratio gives insight into testing discipline. For ATTD, the stories-reopened rate is a killer metric: if stories keep coming back because they didn't actually meet acceptance criteria, ATTD can dramatically reduce that. The TDD adherence formula helps measure whether the team is actually doing TDD or just writing tests after the fact.

📅 Date-Driven Forecasting

"The release date is June 30. How much scope can we deliver?"

Deliverable Scope Available Sprints × Avg Velocity = Max Story Points

Use when the date is fixed (regulatory, contractual, market window)

📦 Scope-Driven Forecasting

"We need all 200 story points. When will we be done?"

Delivery Date Total Story Points ÷ Avg Velocity = Required Sprints

Use when scope is non-negotiable (MVP, compliance requirements)

These are the two fundamental forecasting modes. In practice, most organizations work with a combination — there's both a date and a scope, and the negotiation is about finding the right balance. Walk through the formulas. For date-driven: if we have 6 sprints and velocity of 25, we can deliver about 150 points. For scope-driven: if we need 200 points and velocity is 25, we need 8 sprints. Always present these as ranges, not exact numbers.

📋 Capacity Planning & Communicating Forecasts

📊 Capacity Adjustments

Adjust velocity per sprint for:

  • Team absences (leave, training, onboarding)
  • Holidays and company events
  • Support rotation or on-call duties
  • Always reserve slack for unknowns — planning to 100% capacity guarantees failure

❌ Don't Say

  • "It will be done by April 15."
  • "We need exactly 6 sprints."
  • "We promise to deliver all 40 stories."

✅ Do Say

  • "We forecast completion between April 1-30, with highest confidence in the April 15-22 range."
  • "Based on current velocity, we need 5-7 sprints."
  • "Budget range: $220K-$280K based on scope."
  • "We commit to the top 30 stories and target all 40."
Template: "Based on [data source], we forecast [scope] delivered in [range]. Confidence: [level]. Key risks: [risks]. Next update: [cadence]."
Capacity planning is where the rubber meets the road. A common mistake is using the same velocity for every sprint without adjusting for known capacity changes. The "Don't Say / Do Say" comparison shows the shift from single-point certainty (which stakeholders want) to ranges with confidence levels (which builds trust). The template at the bottom gives participants a script for their next stakeholder meeting. This communication style builds trust over time because you're honest about uncertainty rather than promising and missing.

Walk through the maturity journey from pure estimation to throughput-based forecasting. The accuracy tracking table reveals a universal pattern: larger stories are much harder to estimate accurately. 🔍 When to Stop Estimating

📊 Throughput-Based Forecasting

Instead of estimating story points, simply count stories completed per sprint.

  • Throughput: 8-12 stories per sprint
  • Remaining: 45 stories in backlog
  • Forecast: 4-6 sprints
Works when:
  • Stories are consistently similar in size
  • Team has stable throughput
  • Estimation provides diminishing returns

⚠️ Keep Estimating When...

  • Story sizes vary dramatically (1 pt to 13 pts)
  • Team is new and needs the estimation conversation for shared understanding
  • Stakeholders require estimate-based forecasts
  • Portfolio-level planning needs point-based capacity allocation
The #NoEstimates Insight: Estimation has a cost. When throughput-based forecasting is equally accurate, the estimation overhead can be eliminated. But this only works for mature teams with consistent story sizing.
This is a nuanced topic. The #NoEstimates movement argues that estimation is waste and throughput counting is sufficient. They have a point — for mature teams with well-refined backlogs of similarly-sized stories, counting items is simpler and equally accurate. But it doesn't work for every team. New teams benefit enormously from the estimation conversation — it builds shared understanding and surfaces assumptions. The answer is: use the right approach for your context. Most teams start with estimates and some eventually evolve to throughput-based forecasting.
