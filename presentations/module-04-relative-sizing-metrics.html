📏 Absolute vs. Relative Estimation

Absolute Estimation

  • "This will take 16 hours"
  • Requires detailed task breakdown
  • Precision implies false confidence
  • Varies wildly by individual
  • Anchored to clock time
VS

Relative Estimation

  • "This is about twice as big as that story"
  • Leverages comparison, not analysis
  • Acknowledges inherent uncertainty
  • Team converges on consensus
  • Anchored to known reference work
Key Insight: Relative estimation asks "how big is this compared to what we already know?" rather than "how long will this take in hours?"
Start with a quick poll: "How many of you have been asked for an exact hour estimate and then been held to it?" This resonates with almost everyone. Explain that absolute estimation is deeply ingrained in traditional project management but is fundamentally flawed for knowledge work where uncertainty is high. Relative estimation flips the question -- instead of asking how long, we ask how big compared to something we already understand. This is not about being imprecise; it is about being honestly uncertain while still providing useful planning data.

📏 The Dog Sizing Analogy

Why Comparison Works Better Than Measurement

The Experiment

Imagine you are asked to estimate the weight of five different dog breeds you have never weighed before:

Absolute Approach

"How much does each dog weigh in kilograms?"

  • Chihuahua: 2 kg? 3 kg?
  • Beagle: 10 kg? 15 kg?
  • Labrador: 25 kg? 35 kg?
  • German Shepherd: 30 kg? 40 kg?
  • Great Dane: 50 kg? 70 kg?

Wide variance, low confidence

Relative Approach

"Rank them from smallest to largest and assign relative sizes"

  • Chihuahua: 1 (our baseline)
  • Beagle: 5
  • Labrador: 13
  • German Shepherd: 13
  • Great Dane: 21

High agreement, useful ordering

Result: People reach consensus much faster on relative size, and the ordering is almost always correct -- even when absolute guesses are wildly off.
This is a classic analogy from Mike Cohn. Walk through it conversationally. Ask participants to quickly guess the weight of a Labrador in kilograms, then compare answers -- they will vary enormously. Now ask them to rank the five dogs from smallest to largest -- nearly everyone will agree. The point is that our brains are wired for comparison, not absolute measurement. We can tell that a Labrador is bigger than a Beagle with high confidence, but estimating its exact weight requires specialized knowledge we do not have. Software stories work the same way.

📏 Why Relative Estimation Works

Cognitive Science and Research Evidence

Cognitive Science Backing

  • Weber-Fechner Law: Our perception of stimuli is logarithmic, not linear -- we naturally think in ratios
  • Miller's Law: We can hold 7 +/- 2 items in working memory -- a small set of relative sizes fits perfectly
  • Anchoring Effect: A known reference story provides a stable anchor for comparison

What the Research Shows

Metric Hours Story Points
Estimation time per story 15-30 min 2-5 min
Team agreement rate 30-50% 70-90%
Forecast accuracy (5 sprints) +/- 50% +/- 15%
Impact of team changes Invalidates estimates Velocity self-adjusts
Important: Relative sizing is not less rigorous than absolute estimation -- it is differently rigorous. It trades false precision for honest accuracy.
This slide provides both the scientific grounding and empirical evidence for why relative estimation works. The Weber-Fechner Law explains why Fibonacci numbers feel natural -- they roughly follow a logarithmic progression. Miller's Law tells us that having 7 values in our scale (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21) is ideal for human cognition. Walk through the table row by row. The estimation time difference is dramatic -- instead of debating whether something takes 12 or 16 hours, teams quickly agree it is "about an 8." Forecast accuracy improves because velocity absorbs all the variables that make hour-based estimates unreliable. The self-adjusting nature of velocity is perhaps the most powerful advantage: when team composition changes, velocity naturally shifts to reflect the new team's capacity without re-estimating the entire backlog.

🔢 Developing Story Points

Effort + Complexity + Uncertainty

Definition: A story point is a composite measure that reflects the overall size of a user story, combining effort, complexity, and uncertainty into a single relative number.
E

Effort

How much work is involved? Volume of tasks and activities.

C

Complexity

How intricate is the solution? Algorithms, integrations, edge cases.

U

Uncertainty

How much is unknown? New technology, unclear requirements.

Example

A story might be low effort (simple CRUD form) but high complexity (complex validation rules) and moderate uncertainty (new third-party API). The story point captures all three dimensions in one number.

Story points are the currency of Agile estimation, and understanding what they represent is critical. Emphasize that story points are NOT just effort -- they intentionally blend three dimensions. The beauty of story points is that the team does not need to decompose which factor contributes what -- they compare the story holistically to their reference stories and assign a size. This is why converting story points to hours destroys their value: it strips away the complexity and uncertainty dimensions.

Why Fibonacci? The increasing gaps between numbers reflect the increasing uncertainty in larger items. We can distinguish a 2 from a 3, but not a 14 from a 15 -- so the scale forces us to acknowledge that distinction is meaningless at larger sizes.

Alternative Scales

Some teams use T-shirt sizes (XS, S, M, L, XL) for early roadmap sizing, or powers of 2 (1, 2, 4, 8, 16). The Fibonacci sequence remains the most widely adopted scale in practice.

The modified Fibonacci sequence is 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21 -- sometimes extended to 34 and beyond, though anything above 21 should be split. Walk participants through why each gap matters. The scale reflects the Weber-Fechner Law from the previous slide -- our perception of size differences is logarithmic. A common question is "why not just use 1-10?" The answer is that linear scales encourage false precision: teams will debate whether something is a 6 or a 7, which is a meaningless distinction. Fibonacci forces a meaningful choice: is it a 5 or an 8? That is a real difference.

🔢 Creating a Reference Story Baseline

The Foundation of Consistent Sizing

1

Select

Choose a well-understood story the whole team has completed

2

Assign

Give it a baseline value (typically 3 or 5 points)

3

Calibrate

Size 3-5 additional stories relative to the baseline

4

Document

Create a reference chart the team can revisit

Good Reference Story Traits

  • Recently completed by the current team
  • Moderate size, well understood by all members
  • Representative of typical work

Common Mistakes

  • Using a story only one person worked on
  • Picking an outlier (too easy or too hard)
  • Never updating the reference as the team evolves
The reference story is the most important decision in setting up story point estimation. Without a shared baseline, team members will calibrate differently and points become meaningless. Walk through the four steps. Avoid 1 as the baseline because it leaves no room for smaller stories. Immediately calibrate by sizing 3-5 more stories relative to the baseline. Document these reference stories somewhere visible. This reference set should be revisited every few months as the team's skills and context evolve. New team members should be walked through the reference stories as part of onboarding.

Example reveal: most team members converge on 5, one outlier at 8 explains their concern about an edge case.

Planning Poker was popularized by James Grenning and later by Mike Cohn. Walk through each step deliberately. Step 1: The Product Owner reads the story aloud and provides context. Keep this brief -- 1-2 minutes. Step 2: The team asks clarifying questions, but this is NOT design time. Step 3: Each person privately selects their card. This is critical -- private selection prevents anchoring. Step 4: On the count of three, everyone reveals simultaneously. Step 5: If there is significant divergence (more than 2 Fibonacci numbers apart), the high and low estimators explain their reasoning. Most stories converge in 1-2 rounds. Time-box discussion to 2-3 minutes per story.

🃏 Simultaneous Reveal & Handling Outliers

Why Simultaneous Reveal Matters

Sequential estimation causes anchoring bias -- everyone drifts toward the first number heard. The HiPPO effect (Highest Paid Person's Opinion) amplifies this when a senior voice speaks first.

Simultaneous reveal ensures:
  • Every perspective is captured independently
  • Equal voice for all team members
  • Hidden knowledge surfaces through divergence

When Estimates Diverge

Spread Action
1 step apart (e.g., 5 & 8) Quick discussion, average or take higher
2 steps apart (e.g., 3 & 8) High and low explain; re-vote once
3+ steps apart (e.g., 2 & 13) Deep discussion -- different assumptions exist
The 2-Minute Rule: Limit outlier discussion to 2 minutes. If the team cannot converge after two rounds, take the higher estimate and move on.
The simultaneous reveal is psychologically essential. Daniel Kahneman's research on anchoring shows that even random numbers can influence subsequent estimates. When a senior developer says "this is a 3" first, junior developers will unconsciously pull their estimate toward 3, even if they initially thought 8. The divergence table helps facilitators manage the discussion. When you see a spread of 3 steps or more, the team members have genuinely different understandings of the story. This is gold -- it means Planning Poker is surfacing hidden assumptions before they become problems during development. Use special cards: "?" for unknowns, infinity for "must split."

⚠️ Constraints on Relative Sizing

Story Points Are NOT Comparable Across Teams

  • Team A's "5" is not the same as Team B's "5"
  • Comparing velocities across teams is meaningless and harmful
Anti-Pattern: "Team Alpha delivers 60 points per sprint but Team Beta only does 35" -- this comparison is invalid and damages morale.

Story Points Are NOT Hours

  • Never convert points to hours
  • Never use points for individual performance tracking
  • Points --> velocity --> sprints --> calendar. Never skip steps.

Point Inflation: The Silent Killer

Over time, teams may assign higher point values to the same type of work, making velocity appear to increase without actual productivity gains.

Warning Signs

  • Velocity steadily increasing without process changes
  • Stories that used to be "3" are now routinely rated "5"
  • Team feels pressure to "increase velocity"

Prevention Strategies

  • Maintain reference stories: Regularly check new estimates against baseline
  • Never use velocity as a performance metric
  • Periodic recalibration: Every quarter, re-estimate a few old stories
Story points are a team-internal measure -- they are calibrated to that specific team's skills, experience, and working context. When management compares velocity across teams, they incentivize point inflation and destroy the honesty that makes estimation valuable. Point inflation usually happens when velocity becomes a target rather than a diagnostic. Goodhart's Law applies: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The prevention strategies center on one principle: velocity is a planning tool for the team, not a performance metric for management.

Definition and Fundamentals

Velocity = The total number of story points the team completes (meets Definition of Done) in a single iteration/sprint.

Key Rules

  • Only count "Done" stories: Partially completed work = 0 points
  • Use average velocity for planning: Not the best sprint, not the worst
  • Use last 3-5 sprints: Recency matters more than history
  • Velocity is a range, not a point: "32-38 points" is more honest than "35 points"
Calculation:
Average velocity = (28 + 35 + 31 + 38 + 33 + 36) / 6 = 33.5 ≈ 34 points/sprint

Range: 28 - 38 points/sprint
Velocity is the engine that converts story points into actionable forecasts. Walk through the chart and emphasize that velocity is measured, not predicted. You do not set a target velocity -- you observe what the team actually delivers. Only completed stories count -- if a team finishes 80% of a 13-point story, they get 0 points for that story this sprint. This incentivizes teams to right-size stories and complete them fully. Always present velocity as a range -- saying "we do about 32-38 points per sprint" sets better expectations than a single number.

📈 Using Velocity for Forecasting

Yesterday's Weather

"Yesterday's Weather" principle: The best predictor of how much work a team will complete next sprint is how much they completed in recent sprints.

Release Forecasting Formula

Remaining work: 180 story points

Average velocity: 34 points/sprint

Sprints needed: 180 / 34 = 5.3 sprints

Sprint length: 2 weeks

Estimated delivery: ~11 weeks (6 sprints)

Range-Based Forecast:
Optimistic: 180 / 38 = 4.7 sprints (~10 weeks)
Likely: 180 / 34 = 5.3 sprints (~11 weeks)
Pessimistic: 180 / 28 = 6.4 sprints (~13 weeks)

Velocity Stabilization Pattern

Phase Sprints Typical Pattern
Forming 1-2 High variance, team learning
Norming 3-4 Variance decreasing, patterns emerging
Stabilized 5+ Predictable range, reliable forecasts

Expect velocity to stabilize within 3-5 sprints. Forecasts made before stabilization carry high uncertainty and should include wider ranges.

"Yesterday's Weather" is a deceptively simple but powerful concept. Rather than trying to predict capacity from first principles, we observe what the team actually delivers and project forward. Always present this as a range with optimistic, likely, and pessimistic scenarios using the team's velocity range. This gives stakeholders an honest picture. The stabilization pattern is important to communicate to management early. Do not make commitments based on Sprint 1 velocity. By Sprint 3-5, you will have a reliable enough velocity to make meaningful forecasts.

❌ Consequences of Not Using Relative Measurement

Hour-Based Estimation Pitfalls

The Precision Trap

  • False confidence: "32 hours" sounds precise but is no more accurate than "about a week"
  • Individual variance: A senior developer's 8 hours is a junior's 24 hours
  • Missing factors: Hours ignore complexity, uncertainty, meetings, context-switching
  • Estimation overhead: Detailed hour breakdowns consume significant planning time
The Parkinson Problem: Work expands to fill the time allotted. When you estimate 16 hours, the work takes exactly 16 hours -- regardless of actual complexity.

Sandbagging and Padding

  • Developers add 30-50% "buffer" to protect themselves
  • Managers then cut 20% because "they always pad"
  • Result: a negotiation ritual that produces fiction
  • Neither party trusts the numbers

Commitment vs. Forecast

  • Commitment: "We guarantee delivery by March 15" (almost always wrong)
  • Forecast: "Based on velocity, we expect delivery between March 10-25" (honest and useful)
  • Hour-based estimates get treated as commitments; velocity-based forecasts remain forecasts
This section builds the case for relative estimation by examining what goes wrong without it. The precision trap is insidious: a spreadsheet full of hour estimates looks professional and thorough, but the precision is illusory. The individual variance problem is particularly important -- with story points, both Alice and Bob would agree on the relative size. Sandbagging is a rational response to being held accountable for estimates. The commitment vs. forecast distinction is crucial for organizational culture. Velocity-based forecasts naturally come with ranges, which sets the right expectation with stakeholders.

❌ Case Study: Two Teams, Two Approaches

Team Waterfall Lite

Hour-Based Estimation

  • Spent 3 days creating detailed hour estimates (4,200 hours planned)
  • At month 3, critical integration underestimated by 400%
  • Delivered 8 weeks late despite "precise" planning
  • Team morale: Low -- felt set up to fail

Team Agile Flow

Relative Estimation + Velocity

  • Spent 2 hours in Planning Poker to size 340-point backlog
  • Velocity stabilized at 34 points/sprint after 3 sprints
  • Delivered in 11 sprints -- within the forecasted 10 +/- 2 range
  • Team morale: High -- forecasts were honest and achievable
Key Difference: Team Agile Flow did not estimate more accurately -- they estimated more honestly. Their range-based forecast set appropriate expectations, and velocity self-corrected as they learned more about the work.
Walk through both scenarios side by side. Team Waterfall Lite followed the traditional approach: decompose everything into tasks, estimate hours, build a Gantt chart. When reality diverged from the plan, the entire plan was invalidated. Team Agile Flow invested minimal time in estimation upfront, let velocity emerge from actual delivery, and provided a range-based forecast that proved accurate. The key insight is not that Agile teams are better at estimating -- it is that they structure their estimation approach to account for uncertainty rather than pretending it does not exist. The morale difference is significant: teams that consistently miss "commitments" lose motivation, while teams that regularly meet range-based forecasts build confidence and trust.

📊 Burn-Down and Burn-Up Charts

Sprint Burn-Down Chart

Remaining work (story points) vs. time (days)

Day 1 ....................................... Day 10

  • Shows remaining work trending toward zero
  • Ideal line shows expected pace
  • Deviations signal risk early

Release Burn-Up Chart

Work completed (cumulative) vs. sprints

Sprint 1 .................................. Sprint 8

  • Shows cumulative progress toward scope
  • Scope line reveals scope creep visually
  • Converging lines show projected completion
Burn-Up vs. Burn-Down: Burn-up charts are generally preferred for release tracking because they show both progress AND scope changes. If the scope line moves up, stakeholders can see exactly when and how much scope was added.
The sprint burn-down shows remaining work within a single sprint -- it helps the team self-manage by making daily progress visible. The release burn-up chart is more powerful for stakeholder communication because it shows how much work has been completed and how much total work exists. When scope changes, the scope line moves up, making scope creep visible and quantifiable. Recommend burn-up charts for release-level reporting and burn-down charts for sprint-level team management.

Traffic Light Reporting: Use velocity-based forecasts to generate honest Red/Amber/Green status -- not gut feelings. Green = within range. Amber = at risk. Red = forecast extends beyond target.
The Cumulative Flow Diagram is one of the most information-dense charts in Agile. The key insight is reading the band widths: if the "In Progress" band is widening, work is entering faster than it is leaving -- you have a bottleneck. The release progress snapshot uses velocity to project when the release will be done. The confidence level is derived from the velocity range: if the worst-case velocity still delivers within the target window, it is green. This approach to traffic light reporting is far more honest than subjective status reports -- it removes the "watermelon effect" (green on the outside, red on the inside).

Key Takeaways

📏 Relative Sizing

Humans are better at comparing than measuring. Relative estimation leverages this strength, producing faster consensus and more honest forecasts than hour-based approaches.

🔢 Story Points & Fibonacci

Story points combine effort, complexity, and uncertainty into a single relative measure. Use the modified Fibonacci scale and calibrate with reference stories.

🃏 Planning Poker

Simultaneous reveal prevents anchoring bias. Divergent estimates surface hidden knowledge. Time-box discussions and celebrate disagreement.

⚠️ Constraints & Velocity

Story points are team-specific, not hours, and vulnerable to inflation. Track velocity as a range for forecasting -- never as a performance metric or cross-team comparison.

❌ Without Relative Sizing

Hour-based estimation leads to false precision, sandbagging, and commitment-vs-forecast confusion. Relative sizing is not easier -- it is more honest.

📊 Business Metrics & Value

Burn-up charts, CFDs, and cost-per-point translate estimation data into business intelligence. Optimize for value throughput, not point throughput.

Review each takeaway briefly, connecting back to the module objectives. Emphasize that these techniques work together as a system: story points feed into velocity, velocity feeds into forecasting, and business value metrics feed into prioritization. Implementing only part of the system loses much of the value. Encourage participants to start with Planning Poker and velocity tracking in their next sprint, then gradually add business value metrics as the team matures.
