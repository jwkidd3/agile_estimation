Decomposing Scope

Breaking the big picture into estimable pieces

We begin with scope decomposition. This is the foundational skill for Agile requirements management. In traditional project management, we created a Work Breakdown Structure. In Agile, we have an analogous but more flexible hierarchy: Epics, Themes, Features, User Stories, and Tasks. Let us explore each level and understand why decomposition is essential for estimation accuracy.

📦 The Scope Hierarchy

EPIC
A large body of work — months of effort, spans multiple sprints
▼
THEME
A collection of related stories grouped by business objective
▼
FEATURE
A distinct piece of functionality that delivers user value
▼
USER STORY
A single unit of work completable within one sprint
▼
TASK
Technical implementation steps (hours of work)
Walk through the hierarchy from top to bottom. An EPIC is the largest container - think User Account Management or Payment Processing System. These can span an entire release or more. THEMES group related stories by a business concern - for example, Security Hardening might be a theme that cuts across several epics. FEATURES are distinct capabilities a user can interact with - like Password Reset within the User Account Management epic. USER STORIES are the primary unit of estimation in Agile - small enough to complete in a single sprint. TASKS are the technical to-do items within a story, typically estimated in hours. Emphasize that teams estimate at the story level, not the task level, for sprint planning purposes.

📦 Why Decomposition Matters for Estimation

🎯 The Cone of Uncertainty

  • Large items have 4x variation in estimates
  • Smaller items narrow the range to 1.25x
  • Decomposition is the primary tool for reducing estimation error
Key Insight: You cannot accurately estimate what you do not understand. Decomposition forces understanding.

✅ Benefits of Decomposition

  • Exposes hidden complexity early
  • Enables parallel work streams
  • Makes progress measurable
  • Improves estimation accuracy by 50-70%
  • Facilitates better sprint planning
  • Supports incremental delivery of value
This is a critical slide. The Cone of Uncertainty concept from Steve McConnell shows that early in a project, estimates can vary by a factor of 4x on either side. As we decompose and learn more, that cone narrows. Studies show that breaking a 100-point epic into ten 10-point stories typically reveals that the actual total is somewhere between 80 and 150 points - the act of decomposition itself uncovers hidden work. Ask the class: Have you ever estimated a big feature, started working on it, and realized it was much bigger than you thought? That is the Cone of Uncertainty in action, and decomposition is how we address it.

📦 Rules of Thumb for Story Size

⚠️ Warning: If a user story cannot be completed within a single sprint, it is too large and must be decomposed further. A story that spans sprints defeats the purpose of iterative delivery.
Walk through the table row by row. The key takeaway is that different levels of the hierarchy serve different planning horizons and use different estimation granularity. Epics are estimated very roughly for roadmap planning, perhaps with T-shirt sizes or a simple order of magnitude. Features are sized in aggregate story points for release planning. User stories are the sweet spot for estimation - they should ideally be completable in 1 to 3 days by a single developer or pair. Tasks are optional sub-items that help the team coordinate daily work. The warning at the bottom is critical: if a story is so large that it will not be done in one sprint, it must be split.

Notice how the granularity increases at each level.

Developing the Release Plan

Mapping stories to iterations for predictable delivery

Now that we understand how to decompose scope, the next question is: how do we organize all these stories into a delivery timeline? That is the purpose of the release plan. The release plan is the bridge between the product backlog and the sprint-level execution. It answers the question: Given our current velocity, when can we expect to deliver this set of features?

🗓️ What a Release Plan Contains

📋 Core Components

  • Release goal — the business outcome targeted
  • Prioritized feature list — what is in scope
  • Estimated velocity — points per iteration
  • Number of iterations — sprint count and cadence
  • Story-to-iteration mapping — which stories go where
  • Key milestones and external dependencies
SprintStoriesPoints
Sprint 1S-01, S-02, S-0321
Sprint 2S-04, S-05, S-0619
Sprint 3S-07, S-08, S-09, S-1023
Sprint 4S-11, S-12, S-1320
Total13 stories83 pts
Avg Velocity: ~21 pts/sprint
Release Date: 4 sprints x 2 weeks = 8 weeks
The release plan is NOT a commitment to a specific set of features on a specific date. It is a forecast based on current knowledge. Emphasize the distinction: fixed date means variable scope, fixed scope means variable date. Agile prefers to fix the date and flex the scope. The table on the right shows a simplified release plan: 13 stories totaling 83 points spread across 4 sprints with an average velocity of 21 points per sprint. In practice, the Product Owner would sequence stories by priority so that if we run out of time, the most valuable stories are already delivered.

🗓️ The Release Planning Ceremony

🤝 Participants

  • Product Owner — priorities and business value
  • Scrum Master — facilitation
  • Development Team — estimation and feasibility
  • Stakeholders — constraints and dependencies

⏱️ Typical Duration

  • Half-day for a 3-month release
  • Full day for a 6-month release
  • Revisited every 2-3 sprints

📝 Ceremony Steps

  1. Product Owner presents the release vision and goals
  2. Team reviews the prioritized backlog
  3. Team provides high-level estimates for unestimated items
  4. Calculate expected velocity (historical or forecasted)
  5. Map stories into iterations by priority order
  6. Identify risks, dependencies, and unknowns
  7. Agree on release date or scope boundary
Release planning is a collaborative ceremony. The Product Owner drives priorities, the team provides estimates and identifies technical risks, and stakeholders contribute constraints like regulatory deadlines or external partner dependencies. The ceremony typically takes half a day for a quarterly release. Emphasize step 4: expected velocity. For a new team, you may need to forecast velocity, which we will cover in the Forecasting module. For an established team, use the average of the last 3-5 sprints.

🗓️ Adjusting as Velocity Stabilizes

Key Principle: The release plan is a living document. It should be updated every 2-3 sprints as actual velocity data replaces forecasted velocity.

📉 Velocity Lower Than Expected

  • Re-prioritize: cut lower-value stories
  • Negotiate scope with Product Owner
  • Extend the release timeline (if date is flexible)
  • Investigate impediments slowing the team

📈 Velocity Higher Than Expected

  • Pull in additional stories from the backlog
  • Consider moving the release date earlier
  • Invest in technical debt or quality improvements
  • Validate estimates are not being gamed
⚠️ Anti-pattern: Never inflate velocity by lowering the Definition of Done. A "done" story that needs rework later is a hidden cost that will erode trust and predictability.
This slide addresses the dynamic nature of release planning. After 2-3 sprints, the team will have real velocity data. If velocity is lower than expected, the Product Owner must make scope decisions. This is healthy - it is the Agile inspect-and-adapt cycle applied to planning. If velocity is higher than expected, great, but be cautious. Confirm the team is not cutting corners. Also validate that the stories being completed are genuinely done per the Definition of Done, not just partially implemented.

Leveraging Themes

Organizing stories by business value streams

Themes are a powerful organizational tool that is often underutilized by Agile teams. While epics and stories define WHAT we are building, themes help us understand WHY we are building it and how different stories relate to strategic business objectives.

🏷️ Grouping Stories by Business Theme

What Is a Theme?

A theme is a collection of related user stories that together support a specific business objective or value stream.

  • Themes cut across epics
  • They represent strategic business goals
  • Stories can belong to multiple themes
  • Themes help stakeholders understand investment allocation
Example Themes for an E-Commerce Platform
🛡️ Security Hardening: 2FA, encryption, PCI compliance, session management
📱 Mobile Experience: responsive design, touch gestures, offline mode, push notifications
⚡ Performance: caching, CDN integration, image optimization, lazy loading
📊 Analytics: event tracking, A/B testing, conversion funnels, dashboards
Notice how themes differ from epics. An epic like Checkout is about a specific feature area. A theme like Security Hardening cuts across multiple epics - checkout security, account security, data protection, etc. Themes allow the business to answer questions like How much of our capacity are we investing in security vs new features vs performance? This is crucial for strategic portfolio management.

🏷️ Theme-Based Roadmap and Prioritization

Theme-based planning helps answer the executive question: What are we investing in and why?

INVEST-ing in Good Stories

The six criteria for well-formed user stories

Bill Wake coined the INVEST acronym as a checklist for high-quality user stories. This is arguably the most practical tool in this entire module. A story that satisfies all six INVEST criteria will be dramatically easier to estimate, plan, and deliver.

📖 The INVEST Criteria

I Independent Stories should not depend on other stories for implementation
N Negotiable Details are open for discussion, not locked in a contract
V Valuable Each story delivers tangible value to a user or the business
E Estimable The team has enough information to provide a size estimate
S Small Completable within a single sprint — ideally 1-3 days
T Testable Clear acceptance criteria define when the story is done
Reveal each letter one at a time and briefly explain it. Independent means stories can be developed in any order - no forced sequencing. Negotiable means the story is a conversation starter, not a requirements specification. Valuable means every story should deliver something a user cares about. Estimable means the team understands enough to size it. Small means completable within one sprint. Testable means clear acceptance criteria exist.

📖 INVEST in Detail

✅ Good Examples

  • Independent: "User can reset password via email" — no dependency on other stories
  • Negotiable: "User can filter products" — implementation details TBD in conversation
  • Valuable: "Customer can track order status" — clear user benefit
  • Estimable: "Display top 10 products by rating" — well-understood scope
  • Small: "Add email validation to signup form" — hours of work
  • Testable: "User sees error message for invalid card" — clear pass/fail

❌ Poor Examples

  • Dependent: "Build payment API" then "Build payment UI" — UI cannot work without API
  • Rigid: "Implement exactly 3 dropdown menus with specific hex colors" — over-specified
  • No value: "Refactor database schema" — what user benefit?
  • Unestimable: "Integrate with partner API" — which API? What data?
  • Too large: "Build user management system" — weeks of work
  • Untestable: "Make the app more user-friendly" — how do we verify?
Go through each pair side by side. For Independent: the good example can be built regardless of what else is in the sprint. The bad example creates a forced sequence. For Valuable: the bad example should be reframed in terms of user value - Improve page load time for product search to under 2 seconds explains WHY the refactoring matters. For Estimable: the bad example is too vague - what partner? What endpoints? For Testable: the bad example has no measurable criteria.

📖 The User Story Template

The Standard Format
As a [type of user],
I want [an action or feature],
So that [a benefit or value].

✅ Well-Written Story

As a returning customer,
I want to save my shipping address,
so that I can check out faster on future orders.

Acceptance Criteria:
• User can save up to 5 addresses
• Default address is pre-selected at checkout
• User can edit or delete saved addresses

❌ Poorly-Written Story

As a user,
I want the address feature,
so that it works.

Problems:
• "User" is too vague — which user?
• "Address feature" is ambiguous
• "It works" provides no testable value
• No acceptance criteria

The three-part template - As a, I want, So that - forces the author to think about WHO benefits, WHAT they need, and WHY it matters. The so that clause is often omitted but is the most important part. It connects the story to business value and helps the team understand the intent behind the request. This understanding is crucial for estimation because it helps the team make implementation decisions.

📖 Story Splitting Techniques

When a story is too large, use these patterns to split it:

✂️ Splitting Patterns

  1. By workflow steps
    Search → Filter → Sort → Paginate
  2. By business rules
    Basic validation → Complex validation
  3. By data variations
    Credit card → PayPal → Crypto
  4. By CRUD operations
    Create → Read → Update → Delete
  5. By happy/sad paths
    Success flow → Error handling
⚠️ Avoid These Splits:
  • By architectural layer — "Build the API" / "Build the UI" creates dependencies
  • By technology — "Set up database" / "Write backend" is not user-valuable
  • Arbitrary halves — "Part 1" / "Part 2" is meaningless
💡 Golden Rule: Every split story must deliver a thin vertical slice of user-visible functionality — touching all layers from UI to database.
Story splitting is an essential skill. When a story is estimated at 13 or more points, it usually needs to be split. The five patterns on the left are proven approaches. The anti-patterns on the right are critical. Splitting by architectural layer creates dependencies and delivers no user value until both are complete. This is actually the Hidden Waterfall pattern we will discuss next.

Understanding The Hidden Waterfall

The anti-pattern that undermines Agile delivery

The Hidden Waterfall is one of the most common and damaging anti-patterns in Agile teams. It occurs when a team appears to be doing Agile but the actual work within those sprints follows a waterfall sequence.

💧 Spotting the Hidden Waterfall

The Anti-Pattern: Sprints are organized by activity phase rather than by delivering working software.
Sprint 1
Analysis → Sprint 2
Design → Sprint 3
Coding → Sprint 4
Testing → Sprint 5
Deploy
❌ This is waterfall with sprint-length phases!

🚩 Warning Signs

  • No working software until Sprint 3+
  • Sprint reviews with "nothing to demo"
  • Specialists idle in certain sprints
  • Stories split by layer (API / UI / DB)
  • "We are still in the design phase"

🤔 Why It Happens

  • Team members are specialists, not generalizing specialists
  • Old waterfall habits carry over
  • Stories are too large or layer-based
  • Insufficient cross-functional collaboration
  • Management measures phase completion
This is a very common problem, especially for organizations transitioning from waterfall. The team adopts Agile ceremonies but continues to work in sequential phases. Sprint 1 is all analysis, Sprint 2 is all design, Sprint 3 is coding, Sprint 4 is testing, and Sprint 5 is deployment. They call these sprints but they are actually waterfall phases with a two-week timebox. The result? No working software is delivered until Sprint 3 at the earliest, and often not until Sprint 5.

💧 Strategies for Cross-Functional Iterations

✅ The Correct Approach

Every sprint delivers a vertical slice of working software — all activities happen within each sprint:

Sprint 1: Analyze + Design + Code + Test → Feature A Done ✓
Sprint 2: Analyze + Design + Code + Test → Feature B Done ✓
Sprint 3: Analyze + Design + Code + Test → Feature C Done ✓

🔧 Practical Strategies

  1. Write vertical stories — each story touches all layers
  2. Pair specialists — pair a backend dev with a frontend dev
  3. Invest in T-shaped skills — deep in one area, capable across many
  4. Refine stories ahead — do just-enough analysis 1 sprint ahead
  5. Measure working software — not phase completion
  6. Swarm on stories — whole team finishes one story before starting another
🎯 Goal: Every sprint review should include a live demonstration of new, working functionality that a user could interact with.
The key insight is that all engineering activities - analysis, design, coding, testing - should happen within EVERY sprint, not across sprints. Each sprint delivers a complete, working vertical slice of functionality. The swarm technique is particularly effective: instead of everyone working on their own story in parallel, the whole team focuses on finishing one story at a time. This reduces work-in-progress and increases flow.

Managing the Product Backlog
and Metrics for Grooming

Keeping the backlog healthy and sprint-ready

A well-maintained product backlog is the foundation of effective sprint planning and accurate estimation. If the backlog is a disorganized list of hundreds of items with no refinement, the team cannot plan or estimate effectively. Backlog grooming, also called backlog refinement, is the ongoing process of keeping the backlog in shape.

📚 Backlog Grooming / Refinement

🔄 The Refinement Ceremony

  • Frequency: Once or twice per sprint (mid-sprint is common)
  • Duration: 5-10% of sprint capacity (e.g., 2-4 hours for a 2-week sprint)
  • Participants: PO + Development Team (+ stakeholders as needed)
  • Scrum Guide: Not a formal event, but an essential ongoing activity

📋 Refinement Activities

  • Clarify story details and acceptance criteria
  • Split stories that are too large
  • Estimate or re-estimate stories
  • Re-prioritize based on new information
  • Remove obsolete stories
  • Add newly identified stories

✅ Definition of Ready (DoR)

A story meets the Definition of Ready when:

  • Story follows the user story template
  • Acceptance criteria are written and understood
  • Story has been estimated by the team
  • Story is small enough for one sprint
  • Dependencies are identified and resolved
  • The team can explain the story to a stakeholder
  • Required UX/design assets are available
Backlog refinement is the ongoing process of preparing stories for upcoming sprints. The Scrum Guide recommends spending about 10% of sprint capacity on refinement. The Definition of Ready is the complement to the Definition of Done. While DoD says when is a story finished, DoR says when is a story ready to be pulled into a sprint. A story that does not meet the DoR should not be brought into sprint planning.

📚 Grooming Metrics

MetricTargetWhy It Matters
Story Readiness Rate ≥ 80% % of stories meeting DoR before sprint planning
Refinement Velocity 2-3 sprints ahead How far ahead the backlog is refined
Backlog Depth 2-4 sprints of work Amount of refined, estimated stories ready
Churn Rate ≤ 10% % of stories changed or removed during a sprint
Story Rejection Rate ≤ 5% % of stories not accepted at sprint review
💡 Healthy Backlog Indicators:
  • Top items are detailed, estimated, and small
  • Middle items are partially refined and roughly sized
  • Bottom items are large, unrefined epics and ideas
  • Total backlog is manageable (not 1000+ items)
⚠️ Unhealthy Signs:
  • Sprint planning takes > 2 hours because stories are unclear
  • Team regularly rejects stories mid-sprint
  • Estimates frequently change during the sprint
  • Backlog has hundreds of items no one has looked at in months
These metrics help the team and Scrum Master assess backlog health. Story Readiness Rate is the percentage of stories pulled into sprint planning that already meet the Definition of Ready - target 80% or higher. Refinement Velocity measures how far ahead the team has refined stories. Churn Rate measures how often stories are changed or removed during a sprint - high churn suggests the PO is not prioritizing effectively.

📚 The Backlog Iceberg

✅ Sprint-Ready
Detailed, estimated, acceptance criteria
📝 Refined
Story written, roughly sized
📦 Outlined
Feature-level, needs decomposition
🌀 Raw Ideas
Epics, visions, unvalidated ideas

🎯 The Refinement Gradient

  • Top 10-15%: Fully refined, meets DoR, ready for next 1-2 sprints
  • Next 20-30%: Partially refined, needs estimation and AC finalization
  • Middle 30%: Feature-level descriptions, needs decomposition into stories
  • Bottom 30-40%: Raw ideas, epics, and visions that may never be built
💡 Key Insight: Invest refinement effort proportionally. Spending hours refining stories that might never be built is waste. Focus refinement energy on the top of the iceberg.
The iceberg analogy is a powerful visual for backlog management. The top of the iceberg, above the waterline, represents sprint-ready stories. These are fully refined, estimated, and have clear acceptance criteria. Just below the surface are partially refined stories. Deeper down are feature-level items that need decomposition. At the very bottom are raw ideas and epics that may or may not ever be built. The key insight is to invest refinement effort proportionally. Do not spend hours writing detailed acceptance criteria for a story that is six months away.

Examining Story Metrics
and the Story Scale

Choosing the right scale for estimation

Now we turn to the scales we use for sizing stories. In Module 4 we will dive deep into relative sizing techniques. Here we introduce the most common scales and when to use each one. The choice of scale affects the precision of your estimates and the speed of the estimation process.

📏 Common Story Scales

🔢 Modified Fibonacci Sequence

1 2 3 5 8 13 21 40 100
  • Gaps increase with size — reflects increasing uncertainty
  • No "9" or "17" — prevents false precision
  • Best for sprint-level planning
  • Stories above 13 should be split

👕 T-Shirt Sizing

XS S M L XL XXL
  • Fast, low-effort estimation
  • Great for roadmap and epic-level planning
  • Less precise, intentionally coarse-grained
  • Can be mapped to point ranges later
💡 Why Fibonacci? The increasing gaps reflect a fundamental truth: the larger something is, the less precisely we can estimate it. The difference between a 1-point and a 2-point story is meaningful. The difference between a 20-point and a 21-point story is noise.
Walk through both scales. The modified Fibonacci sequence is the most common scale for story point estimation. The key insight is that the gaps between numbers increase as the numbers get larger. This reflects the Cone of Uncertainty: small stories can be estimated with reasonable precision, but large stories have inherently higher uncertainty. T-shirt sizing is used for quicker, less formal estimation, often at the epic or feature level during roadmap planning.

📏 Story Point Reference Chart

1 XSTrivial changeFix a typo in a labelImplement directly
2-3 SSimple, well-understoodAdd a form field with validationImplement directly
5 MModerate complexityBuild a search filter componentImplement directly
8 LSignificant effort, some unknownsIntegrate a third-party payment APIConsider splitting

⚠️ Important: This reference chart is team-specific. Each team must calibrate their own scale based on their experience, technology stack, and capacity. One team's 5-point story may be another team's 8.
This reference chart provides a starting point for teams establishing their estimation scale. Emphasize that the chart is team-specific. What matters is internal consistency within a team, not cross-team comparisons. A 5-point story for Team A and a 5-point story for Team B may represent very different amounts of work. The Action column is particularly important. Stories at 8 points should trigger the question can we split this? Stories at 13 must be split. Anything at 21 or above is an epic that needs decomposition.

📏 When to Use Which Scale

ContextRecommended Scale
Sprint planningFibonacci
Release planningFibonacci
Roadmap / epic sizingT-Shirt

💡 Guideline: Use Fibonacci when you need precision for near-term planning. Use T-shirt sizing when speed matters more than precision for longer-term planning.
⚠️ Avoid:
  • Linear scales (1-10) — encourages false precision
  • Hours-based estimation for stories — conflates size with duration
  • Mixing scales within the same planning horizon
The choice of scale should match the planning context. For sprint and release planning where you need to calculate velocity and forecast delivery dates, Fibonacci story points give you the numeric precision to do math. For roadmap planning, T-shirt sizing is faster. Avoid linear scales because they encourage false precision. Never estimate stories in hours - hours conflate size with duration and vary based on who is doing the work.

Using Spikes and
"Get Smart" Stories

Reducing uncertainty through time-boxed research

What happens when the team encounters a story they simply cannot estimate? The answer is usually: we need more information. That is where spikes and Get Smart stories come in. These are deliberate, time-boxed investments in learning that reduce uncertainty and enable better estimation.

🧪 What Is a Spike?

💡 Spike Definition

A spike is a time-boxed research or prototyping task designed to answer a specific question or reduce uncertainty about a story or technical approach.

Origin: The term comes from Extreme Programming (XP). Just as a railroad spike is hammered in to test if the ground is solid, an Agile spike tests if an approach is viable.

⏱️ Key Characteristics

  • Time-boxed: Fixed duration (typically 1-3 days)
  • Question-driven: Must answer a specific question
  • Disposable output: Prototype code is thrown away
  • Knowledge is the deliverable — not production code

✅ When to Use a Spike

  • Team cannot estimate a story — too many unknowns
  • New technology or API that no one has used before
  • Architectural decision with multiple viable options
  • Performance feasibility — "Can we process 10K transactions/second?"
  • Third-party integration complexity is unknown

❌ When NOT to Use a Spike

  • The team is just uncomfortable estimating — push through it
  • The question can be answered by a 15-minute conversation
  • As an excuse to avoid committing to an estimate
  • When the story is simply too large — split it instead
Spikes are one of the most valuable but also most misused tools in Agile. A spike is appropriate when the team genuinely cannot estimate because they lack critical information. The key constraint is the time-box. A spike should be 1-3 days maximum. If the question cannot be answered in that time, the question is too broad and needs to be decomposed. The output of a spike is knowledge, not production code. Any prototype code written during a spike should be discarded and rewritten properly.

🧪 "Get Smart" Stories

🧠 What Is a "Get Smart" Story?

A specialized type of spike focused on reducing estimation uncertainty for a specific set of upcoming stories.

Format:
As a development team,
We want to investigate [specific technical question],
So that we can confidently estimate stories [S-14, S-15, S-16].
💡 The Get Smart Workflow:
  1. Identify unestimable stories in backlog
  2. Write a "Get Smart" story with a specific question
  3. Time-box it (1-2 days)
  4. Present findings to team
  5. Re-estimate the originally blocked stories

📋 Example Get Smart Stories

  • "Get Smart on Payment Gateway"
    Investigate Stripe vs Braintree API capabilities, latency, and integration effort. Deliver a recommendation and effort estimate for the payment stories.
  • "Get Smart on Real-Time Updates"
    Prototype WebSocket vs Server-Sent Events for the live dashboard feature. Measure connection limits and browser compatibility.
  • "Get Smart on Data Migration"
    Analyze the legacy database schema and identify transformation rules. Estimate the number of migration scripts needed.
Get Smart stories are a specific variant of spikes with a deliberate focus on enabling estimation. The name comes from the idea that the team needs to get smart about a topic before they can estimate related work. The key difference from a general spike is that Get Smart stories are explicitly tied to specific backlog items. The output is not just knowledge but specifically the ability to estimate previously unestimable stories.

🧪 How Spikes Feed Back Into Estimation

Unestimable Story
❓ "We don't know enough" → Spike / Get Smart
🔍 Time-boxed research → Knowledge Gained
📋 Findings documented → Estimable Stories
✅ Sized with confidence

📋 Spike Deliverables

  • Written findings shared with the whole team
  • Recommendation for technical approach
  • Revised estimates for related stories
  • Identified risks and mitigation strategies
  • New stories discovered during research
📊 Tracking Spikes:
  • Spikes are estimated in hours, not story points (they are time-boxed)
  • They do not count toward velocity (no user value delivered)
  • Track spike frequency per sprint — more than 1-2 suggests backlog needs more refinement
  • Measure spike effectiveness: did the team successfully estimate afterward?
This slide shows the complete feedback loop. An unestimable story triggers a spike. The spike produces knowledge. That knowledge enables confident estimation of the previously blocked stories. For tracking: spikes are time-boxed and estimated in hours, not story points, because they do not deliver user-facing value. They should not count toward velocity. If a team is doing 3+ spikes every sprint, it suggests the backlog is not being refined far enough ahead.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • 📦 Decompose relentlessly: Break epics into themes, features, stories, and tasks. Smaller items yield more accurate estimates and expose hidden complexity.
  • 🗓️ Release plans are living documents: Update them every 2-3 sprints as real velocity data replaces forecasts. Fix the date, flex the scope.
  • 🏷️ Themes connect work to strategy: Use themes to track investment allocation across business value streams and communicate priorities to stakeholders.
  • 📖 INVEST in every story: Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, and Testable. Stories that fail INVEST criteria will undermine estimation accuracy.
  • 💧 Avoid the Hidden Waterfall: Every sprint must deliver working software through vertical slices, not horizontal phase-based work.
  • 📚 Groom the iceberg: Keep the top of the backlog refined and sprint-ready. Use the Definition of Ready as your quality gate.
  • 📏 Choose scales wisely: Fibonacci for sprint/release planning precision, T-shirt sizing for roadmap-level speed.
  • 🧪 Spike before you guess: When you cannot estimate, invest in a time-boxed spike. Knowledge is cheaper than wrong estimates.
