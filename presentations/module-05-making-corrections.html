Day 2 — Responding to Reality

Welcome to Module 5 on Making Corrections. This is a pivotal module because it addresses the reality every Agile team faces: plans change, estimates are imperfect, and the ability to course-correct is what separates high-performing teams from struggling ones. We will cover five key areas: dealing with inaccurate estimates, handling missed iteration goals, managing new and changed requirements, tracking historical trends for continuous improvement, and prioritizing work using relative ROI. The emphasis throughout is that corrections are not failures -- they are a sign of a healthy, responsive Agile process.

Module Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Identify root causes of estimation inaccuracy and apply re-estimation techniques
  • Respond effectively when iteration goals are missed without derailing the project
  • Manage scope changes mid-sprint using disciplined trade-off approaches
  • Use historical trend data to improve forecasting and team predictability
  • Prioritize and sequence work using relative ROI analysis
Walk through each objective and set the expectation that this module is very practical. We will work through real scenarios and participants will get hands-on practice in the lab. Emphasize that all five of these skills are interconnected -- good historical tracking feeds better estimates, which leads to fewer missed goals, which makes scope change management easier.

🔄 Why Estimates Are Wrong (And That's OK)

The Cone of Uncertainty

Early estimates can be off by 4x in either direction. As the project progresses, the cone narrows.

Common Sources of Inaccuracy

  • Anchoring bias in Planning Poker
  • Incomplete understanding of acceptance criteria
  • Underestimating integration complexity
  • Ignoring non-functional requirements
  • Optimism bias ("it'll be quick")

✅ The Agile Mindset

Estimates are not commitments. They are forecasts made with the best information available at the time.

The goal is not perfect estimates -- it is consistently useful estimates that improve over time.

⚠️ Warning Sign

If your estimates are consistently accurate from Sprint 1, you may be padding rather than estimating -- which erodes trust and hides capacity.

Start by normalizing estimation inaccuracy. The Cone of Uncertainty is a well-known concept from software engineering -- at the start of a project, we know the least and our estimates are the most uncertain. That is expected and healthy. Walk through each source of inaccuracy and ask the class if they have experienced these. The key message is that inaccurate estimates are not a personal failing -- they are a natural consequence of working with incomplete information. Our job is to build a system that improves over time, not to demand perfection up front.

🔄 Re-estimation Techniques Mid-Sprint

When to Re-estimate

Scenario Action
Story is larger than expected but completable Accept variance
Story has ballooned in scope Split the story
Technical discovery changes complexity Re-estimate remaining
Blocked by external dependency Escalate & swap

💡 The 50% Rule

If a story is more than 50% through the sprint and less than 50% complete, it is a candidate for immediate re-evaluation.

Do not wait until the last day to acknowledge a problem.

🔧 Swarming

Pull additional team members onto a struggling story to finish it within the sprint. Works best when:

  • Work is parallelizable
  • Team members have context
  • Sprint goal depends on it
This slide covers the practical decision-making process when an estimate proves wrong during a sprint. Walk through the table scenarios one by one. The 50% Rule is a useful heuristic -- if half the sprint is gone and the story is not at least halfway done, it is time to have a conversation. Swarming is a powerful technique but it requires stories that can be worked on by multiple people. Emphasize that the daily standup is the right forum to surface these issues early. Never surprise the team or stakeholders at the end of the sprint.

🔄 Estimation Accuracy Metrics

Measuring Accuracy Over Time

Estimation Accuracy Ratio

Actual Effort / Estimated Effort

  • 0.8 - 1.2 = Healthy range
  • 1.2 - 1.5 = Consistent underestimation
  • > 1.5 = Systemic problem

Track Per Story Size

Teams often estimate small stories accurately but struggle with larger ones. Track accuracy by story point category:

  • 1-3 points: typically accurate
  • 5-8 points: moderate variance
  • 13+ points: high variance (split these!)

📊 When to Adjust vs. Accept Variance

Adjust when:

  • The same type of story is consistently mis-estimated
  • A new technology or domain was introduced
  • Team composition changed significantly

Accept variance when:

  • Errors are random (some over, some under)
  • The overall velocity is still predictable
  • Individual story variance averages out

⚠️ Anti-pattern

Do not re-estimate completed stories retroactively to "fix" your numbers. This destroys the data you need to improve.

This slide is about building a measurement system around estimation quality. The Estimation Accuracy Ratio is simple but powerful -- track it sprint over sprint and look for patterns. The per-story-size breakdown often reveals that the real problem is not estimation in general but estimation of large stories specifically. This naturally leads to the practice of splitting stories smaller, which both improves estimation accuracy and flow. The anti-pattern warning is critical: if you change historical estimates after the fact, you lose the learning signal.

📉 Root Cause Analysis for Missed Goals

The Five Whys of Missed Sprint Goals

Symptom: Team completed 28 of 40 planned points

Why? Two stories took 3x longer than estimated

Why? Integration with legacy system was more complex

Why? No spike was done to assess integration risk

Why? The team assumed it would be similar to a past integration

Why? No one verified that assumption during grooming

Root Cause: Insufficient technical discovery during backlog refinement. This is a process problem, not a people problem.

Common Root Causes

🔴 Estimation Issues

  • Over-committing to velocity
  • Poorly defined acceptance criteria

🟠 Process Issues

  • Too many work-in-progress items
  • Unplanned work / interruptions

🟡 Team Issues

  • Key person absence
  • Skill gap on specific technology
This is one of the most important slides in the module. When sprints are missed, the natural instinct is to blame individuals. The Five Whys technique redirects the team toward systemic causes. Walk through the example slowly and emphasize how each why peels back a layer. The root cause here is a process gap, not a performance issue. On the right side, the three categories help teams structure their retrospective discussions. Estimation issues, process issues, and team issues each require different remedies. Additional items to discuss verbally: Estimation -- ignoring technical debt overhead; Process -- dependency bottlenecks; Team -- context switching overhead. Ask the class which category they see most often in their organizations.

📉 Adjusting Velocity Expectations

The Yesterday's Weather Approach

Best predictor of next sprint's velocity = average of last 3-5 sprints

Velocity Trend Example

Sprint 1: ████████████████████ 20 pts Sprint 2: ██████████████████████████ 26 pts Sprint 3: ███████████████████████ 23 pts Sprint 4: ████████████████████ 20 pts (missed) Sprint 5: █████████████████████████ 25 pts Sprint 6: ████████████████████████ 24 pts ──────────────────────────────────────────── Avg (3-6): 23 pts | Range: 20-26 pts

Plan for the range, not the peak. Commit to 20 pts, stretch to 25 pts.

Handling Unfinished Stories

Option 1: Split and Carry (Preferred)

Create a new story for remaining work. Re-estimate only the remaining portion.

Best when: significant work completed. Credit the done work to the current sprint.

Option 2: Carry Over or Return to Backlog

Move the entire story to the next sprint (if little work done), or send it back for re-prioritization if priorities have shifted.

Keep original estimate for carry-over; re-evaluate value for backlog return.

Yesterday's Weather is a simple but effective heuristic for velocity planning. Rather than aspirational commitments, use your actual track record. The velocity chart shows a realistic pattern -- it fluctuates, and that is normal. The key is to plan for the lower end of the range while allowing stretch goals. For unfinished stories, Option 2 is generally preferred because it gives credit for work done, keeps velocity data clean, and forces the team to think about what remains. Discuss with the class how their teams currently handle unfinished stories and whether they are taking credit for partial work.

📉 Sprint Retrospective: Estimation Focus

Structured Estimation Retro

1. Review estimated vs. actual for each story
2. Identify the 2-3 biggest variances
3. Apply root cause analysis to each
4. Define one actionable improvement
5. Track improvement in next sprint

One improvement per sprint. Trying to fix everything at once fixes nothing.

Communicating Missed Goals

✅ Effective Communication Pattern

  1. State the fact: "We completed 28 of 40 planned points"
  2. Explain the cause: "Integration complexity exceeded our estimates"
  3. Share the impact: "Feature X will be delayed by one sprint"
  4. Describe the action: "We're adding spikes for all integration stories going forward"

🚫 Never Do This

  • Blame individual team members
  • Hide the miss and inflate numbers
  • Promise to "make it up" next sprint
  • Present excuses instead of root causes
This slide addresses two critical skills: running an estimation-focused retrospective and communicating misses to stakeholders. For the retro, the five-step process gives structure to what can otherwise be an unfocused blame session. Emphasize step 4 -- one actionable improvement. Teams that try to fix five things end up fixing none. For stakeholder communication, the four-part pattern is simple and professional. Practice it with the class. The Never Do This section addresses common anti-patterns that destroy trust. Ask participants if they have seen any of these in their organizations and how it affected the team's relationship with stakeholders.

📉 Recovery Strategies for Missed Goals

80% Minimum Sprint Goal Completion Rate Target
2-3 Max Consecutive Misses Before Intervention
10% Velocity Reduction Buffer for Planning

📋 Short-Term Recovery

  • Reduce next sprint commitment by 15-20%
  • Increase focus on highest-priority items
  • Limit work-in-progress strictly
  • Add capacity for unplanned work buffer

🎯 Long-Term Improvements

  • Invest in backlog refinement quality
  • Build spike stories into the cadence
  • Train team on relative estimation techniques
  • Review and tighten Definition of Ready
"A team that misses one sprint goal is learning. A team that misses three in a row has a systemic problem that needs addressing."
This slide provides concrete thresholds and strategies. The 80% sprint goal completion rate is a reasonable target -- if teams are hitting 100% every sprint, they may be under-committing. The 2-3 consecutive miss threshold is when a Scrum Master should escalate and facilitate a deeper investigation. The 10% velocity reduction buffer is a practical way to build slack into planning. Walk through both the short-term and long-term strategies. Short-term fixes address the immediate pain while long-term improvements prevent recurrence. The quote at the bottom reframes the situation: one miss is learning, three in a row requires action.

🔄 Change Management in Agile Context

Agile Manifesto: "Responding to change over following a plan"

But responding to change does not mean accepting chaos. Agile provides a disciplined framework for managing change.

🚫 Traditional Change Control

  • Change Request Board reviews
  • Impact analysis documents
  • Formal approval workflows
  • Weeks of delay

Result: Changes are discouraged, even valuable ones

✅ Agile Change Approach

  • Product Owner makes scope decisions
  • Sprint boundary provides natural gates
  • Backlog is continuously reprioritized
  • Team estimates impact in real time

Result: Valuable changes happen quickly, with discipline

Key Principle: Changes to the backlog are welcome at any time. Changes to the sprint are only welcome under specific, disciplined conditions.

This slide sets up the framework for how Agile handles change differently from traditional project management. The contrast between traditional change control boards and Agile's approach should resonate with participants who have experienced both. The key distinction at the bottom is critical: the backlog is always open for change, but the sprint has boundaries. This is not rigidity -- it is the discipline that makes Agile sustainable. Without sprint boundaries, teams are constantly context-switching and never finishing anything. Ask the class about their experience with change management and where they fall on this spectrum.

🔄 Trading Scope: The Equivalence Rule

⚖️ The Golden Rule of Sprint Scope Changes

Adding = Removing

For every story point added mid-sprint, an equal number of points must be removed.

The "2-for-1" Rule

Why 2-for-1?

When adding work mid-sprint, apply a 2x cost multiplier:

  • Context switching overhead
  • Disruption to flow state
  • Replanning and coordination cost
  • Incomplete story setup (no refinement)

To add a 5-point story mid-sprint, remove 10 points of planned work.

Example Trade Scenario

📥 Incoming Request

"Critical bug fix needed" -- estimated at 3 points

📤 Trade-off (2-for-1)

Remove 6 points of lowest-priority sprint items:

  • Story A: UI polish (3 pts) → back to backlog
  • Story B: Report enhancement (3 pts) → back to backlog

✅ Net Result

Sprint commitment reduced by 3 points, but the highest-value work is still prioritized.

The equivalence rule and the 2-for-1 rule are two different levels of discipline. At minimum, teams should follow the equivalence rule: adding equals removing. More mature teams apply the 2-for-1 rule because mid-sprint additions have hidden costs. The example makes this concrete. A 3-point bug fix does not just cost 3 points of capacity -- it costs the disruption of the team's flow, the replanning effort, and the context-switching tax. By requiring the Product Owner to remove 6 points of work, we create the right economic signal: mid-sprint changes are expensive, so only truly urgent items should be added. This is not about being inflexible -- it is about making the true cost visible.

🔄 Impact Analysis for Mid-Sprint Changes

Decision Framework

🔥 Emergency

Production down or security breach

Action: Add immediately, apply 2-for-1

⏰ Urgent

Critical bug or key customer issue

Action: PO approves trade, else next sprint

📋 Important

Feature request or strategic initiative

Action: Add to backlog for next sprint

💭 Nice to Have

Polish, optimization, minor improvements

Action: Backlog, schedule when capacity allows

Mid-Sprint Change Checklist

Before Adding to Sprint

  1. Is this truly sprint-urgent? 🤔
  2. What is the estimated effort?
  3. What will be removed to compensate?
  4. Has the PO approved the trade-off?

⚠️ Red Flags

  • More than 2 mid-sprint additions per sprint
  • Additions without corresponding removals
  • "Everything is urgent" mentality
  • Bypassing the team in scope decisions
The quadrant matrix gives teams a quick classification tool for incoming changes. Walk through each quadrant and emphasize that only the Emergency quadrant warrants immediate sprint disruption. Everything else either goes through the Product Owner's trade-off process or simply goes to the backlog. The checklist on the right provides a practical tool teams can use when evaluating mid-sprint changes. Additional checklist items to discuss verbally: "Does the team agree they can absorb it?" and "Does it affect the sprint goal?" The red flags section addresses organizations that treat every request as urgent -- this is a cultural problem that needs to be surfaced and addressed. If everything is urgent, nothing is urgent. Track the number of mid-sprint additions as a metric and discuss it in retrospectives.

🔄 Integrating Changes Through Backlog Refinement

Continuous Refinement Cadence

📅 Mid-Sprint: Refine next sprint's stories
🔍 Ongoing: PO triages new requests to backlog
⚖️ Sprint Planning: Commit based on refined stories only

💡 The Refinement Rule

A team should always have 2-3 sprints worth of refined stories ready in the backlog. This absorbs change without disruption.

Managing Requirement Volatility

Volatility Level Strategy
Low (<10% change/sprint) Standard refinement cadence is sufficient
Medium (10-25% change/sprint) Increase refinement frequency; add capacity buffer
High (>25% change/sprint) Shorten sprints; consider Kanban hybrid; address root cause

🎯 Proactive Change Absorption

  • Reserve 10-15% capacity for unplanned work
  • Use "stretch goals" for additional items if no disruptions occur
  • Track unplanned work ratio as a health metric
This slide shifts from reactive change management to proactive change absorption. The refinement cadence ensures the team always has well-understood work ready to go, which reduces the pressure to add unrefined stories mid-sprint. The volatility table helps teams calibrate their approach based on how much change they actually experience. If a team is seeing more than 25% of their sprint scope change regularly, the problem is not the changes themselves -- it is likely a misalignment between the Product Owner and stakeholders on priorities, or an environment that is genuinely too dynamic for fixed-length sprints. In those cases, a Kanban or Kanban-Scrum hybrid may be more appropriate.

Velocity trend charts are one of the most misused tools in Agile. This slide teaches participants how to read them correctly. 📈 Cycle Time and Lead Time Trends

Definitions

Lead Time: From request creation to delivery
(Measures responsiveness to stakeholders)

Cycle Time: From work started to delivery
(Measures team efficiency)

🔍 The Gap Tells a Story

Metric Example
Lead Time 14 days
Cycle Time 4 days
Wait Time 10 days (71%!)

Items spend more time waiting than being worked on. Focus on reducing wait time.

Using Cycle Time for Estimation

📊 Percentile-Based Forecasting

Instead of estimating story points, use historical cycle time:

  • 50th percentile: 3 days Likely
  • 85th percentile: 7 days Confident
  • 95th percentile: 12 days Almost certain

✅ Benefits Over Story Points

  • Based on actual data, not estimates
  • No estimation meetings needed
  • Naturally includes all overhead
  • Easier for stakeholders to understand
Cycle time and lead time are flow-based metrics that complement or even replace story point estimation for mature teams. The gap between lead time and cycle time is particularly revealing -- in most organizations, items spend far more time waiting in queues than being actively worked on. This means the biggest improvement opportunity is usually in reducing wait time, not in making developers work faster. The percentile-based forecasting approach is powerful: instead of asking the team to estimate a story, you look at historical data and say with confidence how long similarly-sized stories have taken. This requires good data hygiene but produces very reliable forecasts. Prerequisite to mention: this approach requires consistent tracking of start/finish dates and at least 30+ data points for meaningful percentiles.

💰 Calculating Relative ROI

ROI = Business Value / Effort

Both measured in relative units (not dollars or hours)

Assigning Business Value

Value Factors to Consider

  • Revenue impact: Direct or indirect
  • Cost reduction: Operational savings
  • Risk reduction: Compliance, security
  • User satisfaction: NPS or retention impact
  • Strategic alignment: Supports key objectives

Use the same scale as story points (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21) for business value. This keeps the math simple and leverages relative sizing skills.

Example Calculation

Feature Value Effort ROI
Search upgrade 13 5 2.6
New dashboard 8 13 0.6
Email notifications 5 3 1.7
Admin panel 3 8 0.4
API v2 21 21 1.0

Prioritization order: Search upgrade (2.6) > Email notifications (1.7) > API v2 (1.0) > New dashboard (0.6) > Admin panel (0.4)

Relative ROI is a powerful prioritization technique because it combines two pieces of information the team already has: business value (from the Product Owner) and effort (from the development team). Walk through the formula and the example. Note how the search upgrade, despite not having the highest absolute value, has the highest ROI because it delivers a lot of value for relatively little effort. The new dashboard has high value but also high effort, resulting in a low ROI. This does not mean we never build the dashboard -- it means we should build the higher-ROI items first. Ask the class to identify which items they would schedule first and why.

💰 Sequencing for Dependencies and Risk

Beyond ROI: Sequencing Factors

🔗 Technical Dependencies

Some high-ROI items cannot start until prerequisites are complete.

  • Map dependencies and identify the critical path
  • Schedule enablers early even if standalone ROI is low

⚠️ Risk-First Approach

Schedule highest-risk items early to fail fast and learn.

  • Unknown technology? Do it in Sprint 1-2
  • Complex integration? Spike it early

Weighted Scoring Model

This slide extends beyond pure ROI to address the reality that sequencing decisions involve multiple factors. Walk through the dependency and risk considerations on the left. The critical insight is that an enabler story with low standalone ROI might be the most important thing to do if it unblocks three high-ROI stories. Additional risk-first item to mention verbally: regulatory requirements should also be validated early. The weighted scoring model on the right provides a more sophisticated approach. Walk through the example: Feature A has better ROI but Feature B scores higher overall because of its risk reduction and dependency unlocking value. The weights should be agreed upon by the team and PO and adjusted based on the project phase. Early in a project, risk reduction and dependency unlocking might be weighted higher; later, ROI and strategic alignment might dominate.

💰 Balancing ROI with Strategic Objectives

The Portfolio View

🎯 Sprint Allocation Strategy

Balance your sprint portfolio across categories:

New Features 60% Tech Debt 20% Bugs 15% Ops 5%

Adjust ratios based on project phase and health metrics.

⚠️ The Tech Debt Trap

Pure ROI-based prioritization will never prioritize tech debt because it has no direct business value. But ignoring tech debt reduces future ROI by slowing delivery.

Solution: Reserve a fixed percentage of capacity for tech debt in every sprint.

Strategic Alignment Check

📋 Quarterly Review Questions

  1. Are our highest-ROI items aligned with company OKRs?
  2. Is our tech debt allocation sustainable?
  3. What would we stop doing if forced to cut 20%?

🔄 Continuous Rebalancing

Signal Response
Velocity declining Increase tech debt allocation
Bug rate rising Invest in quality practices
Customer churn up Prioritize retention features
Competitor launching Accelerate strategic features
This slide brings everything together at the portfolio level. ROI-based prioritization is powerful but incomplete. Smart teams balance their work across categories: new features, technical debt, bug fixes, and operational work. The allocation percentages shown are guidelines -- each team should calibrate based on their situation. The tech debt trap is one of the most common strategic mistakes in Agile teams: because tech debt reduction has no direct business value, it never wins in a pure ROI comparison. But the compounding effect of ignored tech debt is devastating -- it is like ignoring maintenance on a building. The quarterly review questions and continuous rebalancing table give teams practical tools for maintaining strategic alignment while still leveraging ROI for tactical prioritization. Additional quarterly review questions to discuss verbally: "Are we investing enough in risk reduction?" and "Are we building for this quarter or next year?"

  • 📈 Scenario 4: Given 8 sprints of velocity data, build a control chart and identify trends, outliers, and planning recommendations.
  • 💰 Scenario 5: Prioritize a backlog of 10 features using relative ROI, then adjust for dependencies and risk using the weighted scoring model.
This lab is the heart of the module. Divide participants into teams of 4-5. Each team will rotate through all five scenarios in 15-minute rounds. Provide pre-built scenario cards with the relevant data for each exercise. For Scenario 1, give teams a sprint board with stories in various states of completion. For Scenario 2, provide a sprint history with velocity data showing the consecutive misses. For Scenario 3, assign one person as the Product Owner who must make the trade-off decision. For Scenario 4, provide actual velocity data in a spreadsheet and have teams create the control chart. For Scenario 5, provide a backlog with value and effort estimates. After all rotations, bring the group back together for a 15-minute debrief where each team shares their most surprising insight.

🎯 Key Takeaways

🔄 Inaccurate Estimates Are Expected

Build systems to detect and respond to estimation errors early. Use the 50% Rule, swarming, and story splitting to stay on track.

📉 Missed Goals Need Root Causes

Apply the Five Whys. Communicate with stakeholders transparently. Focus on one process improvement per sprint.

🔄 Scope Changes Require Discipline

Adding equals removing. Apply the 2-for-1 rule for mid-sprint changes. Protect sprint boundaries while welcoming backlog changes.

📈 Historical Data Drives Improvement

Track velocity trends, cycle times, and control charts. Use data to forecast, not to judge. Account for team composition effects.

💰 ROI Guides Prioritization

Calculate relative ROI to sequence work. Use the ROI matrix for visual alignment. Balance with dependencies, risk, and strategic goals.

🔧 Corrections Are Healthy

The ability to make corrections is a core Agile strength. Embrace the feedback loop: estimate, execute, measure, adjust, repeat.

Review each takeaway and connect it back to the specific content covered. Ask the class which takeaway resonated most with them. Emphasize the final point: making corrections is not a sign of failure, it is the defining characteristic of an Agile team. A team that never corrects is not perfect -- it is ignoring reality. Remind participants that these skills build on each other: good historical tracking leads to better estimates, which reduces missed goals, which creates stability that makes scope management easier, which produces better ROI outcomes.
