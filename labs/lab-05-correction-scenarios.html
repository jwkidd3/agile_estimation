🔄 Lab 5: Correction Scenarios Workshop

Module 5 — Agile Project Estimation | Duration: 65 minutes (50 min work + 15 min presentations) | Style: Group Workshop

🎯 Lab Objectives

  • Practice responding to estimation challenges as a team — corrections require collective ownership
  • Apply correction techniques collaboratively to realistic project scenarios
  • Calculate relative ROI through group consensus to prioritize backlog items
  • Build team confidence in stakeholder communication during course corrections

👥 Group Setup

Form groups of 4-5 people. Course corrections in Agile are never a solo activity — they require the whole team to diagnose, decide, and communicate. Every exercise in this lab is done as a group. Assign roles:

  • Scrum Master: Facilitates discussion, ensures the group considers all angles
  • Product Owner: Advocates for business impact and stakeholder communication
  • Tech Lead: Provides the technical perspective on feasibility and trade-offs
  • Team Members: Challenge ideas, provide diverse viewpoints

Tools & Presentation

Choose your tools: Your team decides how to work through the scenarios — whiteboard, spreadsheet, digital tool, paper, or any combination. Use whatever works best for your group.

Class presentation: At the end of the lab, each team presents their scenario responses and analysis to the class (3-5 minutes per team).

1 Scenario Response Drills

20 minutes

For each scenario, work as a group to determine the appropriate response. Each role should contribute their perspective — the Scrum Master on process, the Product Owner on business impact, and the Tech Lead on technical options. Document your team's action plan.

Scenario A: The Blow-Up Story

Mid-sprint, your team discovers that a 5-point story ("integrate with payment gateway") is actually far more complex than expected. The payment API documentation is outdated, and the team now estimates 21 points of work remain. Sprint capacity is 24 points total, and the team has already committed to 22 points this sprint.

What actions do you take? What do you communicate and to whom?

Scenario B: The Velocity Dip

Your team has maintained a steady velocity of 30 points/sprint for 4 sprints. Sprint 5 delivered only 18 points, and Sprint 6 delivered 20 points. A major release is due in 4 sprints with 100 points remaining in the backlog.

What is your root cause investigation plan? How do you adjust the release forecast?

Scenario C: The Scope Bomb

The CEO returns from a conference excited about AI chatbot capabilities and demands that a conversational AI feature be added to the product. The team estimates this at 40 story points. The release is 3 sprints away with the backlog fully committed at current velocity.

How do you handle this request? What trade-offs do you present?

Scenario D: The Persistent Over-Committer

For 3 sprints in a row, the team has committed to 28 points but only completed 20-22 points. Unfinished stories keep rolling over. The Product Owner is frustrated that planned features keep slipping.

What systemic changes do you recommend? How do you reset expectations?

Analysis Questions

1. Calculate the accuracy percentage for each sprint. What pattern do you see?

2. What happened between Sprint 5 and Sprint 7? What likely caused the disruption?

3. Is there a correlation between mid-sprint story additions and accuracy? Between defects and velocity?

4. What velocity would you recommend this team commit to for Sprint 11? Explain your reasoning.

4 Team Presentation to Class

15 minutes (3-5 min per team)

Each team presents to the class. Cover:

  • Your action plan for the most challenging scenario — what would you do and how would you communicate it?
  • Your ROI-prioritized backlog and any cases where you overrode the math
  • Your key insight from the historical trend analysis

Other teams should challenge the presented approach — would they handle the scenarios differently?

💡 Discussion Points

  • In Scenario A, what would happen if the team tried to "hero" through and keep all commitments?
  • How does ROI-based prioritization differ from stakeholder-driven prioritization?
  • What is the most important leading indicator that a team's estimation is improving?
  • When should a team stop estimating and simply count stories (use throughput instead)?
