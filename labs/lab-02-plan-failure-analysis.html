💥 Lab 2: Plan Failure Analysis

Module 2 — Agile Project Estimation | Duration: 60 minutes (45 min work + 15 min presentations) | Style: Group Workshop

🎯 Lab Objectives

  • Collaboratively identify common failure patterns in project plans
  • Analyze real-world case studies as a team to diagnose planning failures
  • Work together to transform a traditional plan into an Agile plan
  • Practice creating adaptive plans through group consensus

👥 Group Setup

Form groups of 4-5 people. Planning failures are rarely visible to just one person — it takes a team to spot the warning signs. Every exercise in this lab is done as a group. Assign roles:

  • Facilitator: Leads the discussion and ensures all voices are heard
  • Scribe: Documents the group's analysis and conclusions
  • Devil's Advocate: Challenges the group's assumptions and pushes deeper analysis
  • Presenter: Shares findings with the class

Tools & Presentation

Choose your tools: Your team decides how to collaborate — whiteboard, sticky notes, spreadsheet, digital tool, paper, or any combination. Use whatever works best for your group.

Class presentation: At the end of the lab, each team presents their key findings to the class (3-5 minutes per team).

1 Plan Failure Autopsy

15 minutes

As a group, read the following project scenario and collaboratively identify every planning failure you can find. Each member should contribute at least one failure pattern — different perspectives will surface different issues.

Scenario: Project Phoenix

A 50-person company decided to rebuild their e-commerce platform. The VP of Engineering created a 12-month project plan based on the following approach:

  • The VP estimated all tasks personally, assigning hours to each developer
  • No buffer was added because "we need to be aggressive with the timeline"
  • Requirements were documented in a 200-page specification upfront
  • All features were marked as "must-have" by the business stakeholders
  • The plan assumed 8 productive hours per developer per day
  • Testing was scheduled for the final 2 months
  • The project was estimated to cost $2M and take exactly 12 months
  • No milestones or checkpoints were planned before month 10
  • Two key developers left at month 3; replacements were expected to be "fully productive in 2 weeks"
  • Scope changes from the CEO were accepted at month 6 without adjusting the timeline

Result: The project delivered 18 months late, 80% over budget, and missing 30% of the originally planned features.

2 Transform the Plan

15 minutes

As a group, redesign Project Phoenix using Agile planning principles. Each member should own one or two planning elements and present their approach to the group for discussion and refinement. The goal is group consensus on every element — just like a real Agile planning session.

Your Agile Plan Should Include:

Planning Element Your Approach
Release Strategy
(How many releases? What cadence?)
Sprint Length & Ceremonies
(What cadence? What meetings?)
Estimation Approach
(Who estimates? What method?)
Scope Management
(How to handle the "everything is must-have" problem?)
Quality Strategy
(When does testing happen?)
Risk Management
(Checkpoints, buffers, escalation?)
Stakeholder Communication
(How often? What format?)

4 Team Presentation to Class

15 minutes (3-5 min per team)

Each team presents to the class. Cover:

  • Your team's failure autopsy — which patterns did you find most critical?
  • Your redesigned Agile plan for Project Phoenix
  • The top 3 failure patterns from your combined real-world experiences

After each presentation, other teams may challenge or build on the analysis.

💡 Discussion Points

  • Which failure pattern is most dangerous and why?
  • How does an Agile plan protect against the failures identified in Project Phoenix?
  • What organizational changes are needed to support Agile planning?
  • How would you pitch the transition from traditional to Agile planning to a skeptical executive?
